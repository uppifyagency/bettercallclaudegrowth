# Patterns & Techniques — Competing Against Luck

## Find the job (the milk-shake method)
**When to use**: before designing or improving anything.
**How**: stop studying the product; reconstruct the *hiring decision*. Ask "What job did you hire this for? What did you fire to do it? What else did you consider — including doing nothing?" Separate distinct jobs the same product serves (morning vs. afternoon shake) — each needs its own design.
**Trade-offs**: qualitative and slow; you must observe real circumstances, not run feature focus groups.

## Job-hunting checklist (the five hunting grounds)
**When to use**: to source new opportunities cheaply.
**How**: look in (1) **your own life** (a job close to home); (2) **nonconsumption** (who hires nothing?); (3) **workarounds/compensating behaviors** (what are people hacking together?); (4) **negative jobs** (what do people refuse to do?); (5) **unusual uses** (off-label uses of your product). Watch behavior, not stated preference.
**Trade-offs**: behavioral observation takes patience; survey shortcuts give you products, not jobs.

## Write the job as a sentence
**When to use**: to lock the definition at the right altitude.
**How**: "When **[circumstance]**, help me **[progress, a verb]**, so I can **[deeper aim]**" — then name the functional, social, *and* emotional dimensions. Use **verbs and nouns**, never adjectives/adverbs. Check the level of abstraction: broad enough to see the true competitive set, narrow enough to guide design.
**Trade-offs**: too broad → can't act; too narrow → blind to competition and to social/emotional needs.

## Forces of Progress worksheet
**When to use**: to diagnose why a switch is or isn't happening.
**How**: map all four forces for the customer:
- **Push** — what makes the status quo intolerable (frustrations, triggering events)?
- **Pull** — what attracts them to the new solution?
- **Habit** — what inertia keeps them where they are?
- **Anxiety** — what choosing-anxiety and using-anxiety hold them back?
A switch needs **push + pull > habit + anxiety**. Find the *binding constraint* and act on it — usually the cheapest win is **reducing anxiety/habit**, not adding features.
**Trade-offs**: most teams over-invest in pull and ignore the two opposing forces.

## Storyboard the struggle
**When to use**: in customer interviews, to hear what they don't say.
**How**: rebuild the timeline like a documentary — first thought → passive looking → active looking → decision → Big Hire → Little Hire(s). Capture emotions and the exact struggling moment, not an attribute ranking.
**Trade-offs**: depth over breadth — a handful of deep reconstructions beats hundreds of surveys.

## Big Hire / Little Hire audit
**When to use**: to check whether the job is truly being done.
**How**: separate the purchase (Big Hire) from actual repeated use (Little Hire). Bought-but-unused = the real job isn't getting done; fix the experience, not the sale.
**Trade-offs**: requires post-purchase observation, which ops dashboards rarely surface.

## Job spec → product résumé
**When to use**: moving from insight to design.
**How**: write the **job spec** (dimensions, trade-offs, competing set, obstacles/anxieties to remove). Then design the product as a **résumé** that answers it line-by-line — engineering away obstacles and anxieties as first-class features, and building the **experiences of purchase and use** that deliver the social/emotional job.
**Trade-offs**: experiences are costlier than features but far harder for rivals to copy.

## Build (and protect) a purpose brand
**When to use**: once you reliably nail a job.
**How**: tie the brand to the job until they're synonymous ("I'll Uber there"). Keep the brand's promise narrow and true to the job; resist diluting it with surface-growth extensions.
**Trade-offs**: a purpose brand commands premium prices but is fragile — off-job extensions blur it.

## Integrate around the job
**When to use**: to build a durable moat.
**How**: identify everything that must coordinate to do the job well, then build **processes** across functional silos to deliver it (Mayo Clinic). Make sure you can *describe the work as a process* — if you can't, you can't deliver the job reliably.
**Trade-offs**: cuts against the org chart; needs leadership to fund cross-silo integration.

## Guard against the three fallacies
**When to use**: continuously, especially as you scale.
**How**: (1) deliberately fund **passive job data** so abundant **active ops data** doesn't crowd it out; (2) test every growth move — does it deepen the core job or just sell more stuff (**surface growth**)?; (3) invite disconfirming evidence to beat **conforming data**. Ask of every metric: "Does this measure the customer's progress, or my internal activity?"
**Trade-offs**: the disciplined choice is always the harder, messier one.

## Run a jobs-focused organization
**When to use**: to keep thousands of decisions aligned.
**How**: state the job as **commander's intent**; empower distributed decisions; route resources to job-progress; and **measure progress on the customer's job** (what gets measured gets done). Replace vague mission statements with an operational job definition.
**Trade-offs**: requires trusting frontline judgment over rigid rules.
