# Chapter 3: Jobs in the Wild

## Core Idea
Real jobs are messy, complex, and often hidden inside what looks like indifference. The richest opportunities sit in **nonconsumption** — people who are getting the job done badly or not at all — and seeing them requires building the whole demand-side picture, not just polishing the product.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Competing against nonconsumption**: the most fertile ground is not stealing share from rivals but unlocking people who currently hire *nothing* (or a clumsy workaround). They have the job but no acceptable candidate.
- **The full complexity of a job**: a real job bundles functional, social, and emotional dimensions *plus* the obstacles, anxieties, and habits surrounding it. You must map all of it, not just the headline function.
- **Southern New Hampshire University (SNHU)**: reframed the online-degree job. The applicant isn't a fresh 18-year-old "finding themselves" — it's a 30-something juggling work, kids, and debt who needs to *make tangible progress in life fast*. SNHU redesigned response time (minutes, not weeks), financial-aid clarity, and adviser support around *that* job — and grew explosively.
- **Airbnb**: succeeded by nailing the social/emotional job (belong anywhere, travel like a local, trust a stranger's home) — not merely "a cheaper hotel room."

## Key Concepts
- **The demand-side view**: companies obsess over the supply side (their product, ops, competitors). Jobs live on the demand side — in the customer's struggle — and that's where you must look.
- **Hidden jobs hide in plain sight**: people accept bad solutions and stop complaining, so surveys show "satisfaction." The struggle is still there; it's just gone quiet.
- **A job has a context, not just a customer**: the same person hires differently across circumstances.

## Mental Models
- Look where people are **MacGyvering** a fix or simply abstaining — that's a job with no good candidate, i.e. open territory.
- Treat the absence of complaint as a clue, not an all-clear: silence often means resignation, not satisfaction.

## Anti-patterns
- Defining your market by your product category and therefore only seeing existing competitors — you miss the larger pool of nonconsumers.
- Designing for a stereotyped customer (the "traditional student") instead of the real circumstance (the working adult under time and money pressure).

## Key Takeaways
1. The biggest growth is usually in **nonconsumption** — find the job people can't currently get done.
2. Map the **whole** job: functional + social + emotional + the surrounding obstacles and anxieties.
3. Winning means seeing the **demand side** clearly enough to redesign the entire experience around the customer's progress (SNHU, Airbnb).
