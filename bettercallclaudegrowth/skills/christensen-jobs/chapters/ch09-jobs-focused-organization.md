# Chapter 9: The Jobs-Focused Organization

## Core Idea
A clearly understood job can become the organizing principle of the whole company — a kind of **commander's intent** that lets everyone, at every level, make the right call without escalating. Make the job, not the function, the thing the organization is built and measured around.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The job as commander's intent**: militaries give a clear *intent* so soldiers improvise correctly when plans meet reality. A well-articulated job does the same in a company — it aligns thousands of independent decisions toward the customer's progress without micromanagement.
- **Four benefits of a jobs-focused organization**:
  1. **Distributed decision-making** — employees make good, autonomous judgment calls because they know the job they're serving (SNHU advisers empowered to *remove friction* for working adults).
  2. **Resource optimization** — investment naturally flows toward what advances the job, away from what doesn't.
  3. **Inspiration & meaning** — people are energized by a customer job (progress in someone's life), not by abstract financial targets.
  4. **Better measurement** — you measure **progress on the customer's job**, not internal proxies. "What gets measured gets done," so measure the right thing.
- **OnStar** ("peace of mind" — the job, not "telematics features") and **Deseret News** (reorganized around the job its readers really hired it for) illustrate building the org and its metrics around the job.

## Key Concepts
- **Mission statements vs. the job**: most mission statements are too vague to guide a frontline decision; a crisply specified job is operational — it tells a clerk what to do *right now*.
- **What gets measured gets done**: align metrics to job progress, or the organization will optimize whatever proxy you happen to track (and drift, per Ch 8).
- **Culture as memory of the job**: when "how we do the customer's job" is repeated until it's instinctive, culture itself keeps the firm on the job.

## Mental Models
- Give the organization an **intent (the job)**, not just instructions — then trust distributed judgment to fill the gaps.
- Audit your **metrics and incentives**: do they reward progress on the customer's job, or internal activity? Change what you measure to change what gets done.

## Anti-patterns
- Governing through **rigid rules and approvals** instead of a shared job — frontline staff can't adapt and the experience degrades.
- **Vague mission statements** that sound noble but can't resolve a real frontline trade-off.
- Measuring **functional/financial proxies** that quietly pull the company off the customer's job.

## Key Takeaways
1. A clearly understood **job functions as commander's intent**, enabling aligned, distributed decisions.
2. Organizing around the job yields **four benefits**: distributed decisions, resource optimization, inspiration, and better measurement.
3. **What gets measured gets done** — measure progress on the customer's job, embed it in culture, and the organization stays on track.
