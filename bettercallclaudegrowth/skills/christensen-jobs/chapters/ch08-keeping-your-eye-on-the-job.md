# Chapter 8: Keeping Your Eye on the Job

## Core Idea
Even companies founded on a clear job drift away from it as they grow — not from incompetence but because three predictable data fallacies quietly substitute the *product* and *operations* for the *job*. Knowing the fallacies is how you stay locked on.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Three Fallacies of Innovation Data**:
  1. **The Fallacy of Active vs. Passive Data** — once a product ships, the firm generates abundant **active data** (operations, sales, efficiency — easy to collect, framed in the company's categories). This crowds out the rich, qualitative **passive data** about the customer's *job and circumstance* (hard to collect, no spreadsheet column). Managers manage the data they have, so the job fades from view. *The map quietly replaces the territory.*
  2. **The Fallacy of Surface Growth** — to grow, firms sell **more products to existing customers** (line extensions, new categories, adjacencies). This "surface growth" feels like progress but dilutes focus on the original job and erodes the purpose brand. Chasing the *company's* need to grow displaces the *customer's* job.
  3. **The Fallacy of Conforming Data** — managers unconsciously **shape data to confirm beliefs they already hold**. Data has no voice; people give it one, and they tend to make it say what fits the prevailing model.
- **"People want a quarter-inch hole, not a quarter-inch drill"** (Levitt) — and the hole is itself only a proxy for deeper progress. Keep laddering up to the real job; V8 found growth by reframing itself against the *vegetables-you-should-eat* job, not the *juice* category.

## Key Concepts
- **Active data is seductive**: it's precise, abundant, and quantifiable — and about the wrong thing (your operations, not the customer's struggle).
- **Growth pressure is the enemy of focus**: the imperative to grow pushes firms toward surface growth and away from the job that made them great.
- **Beliefs masquerade as analysis**: without discipline, "the data shows…" often means "the data was arranged to show what we expected."

## Mental Models
- Ask of every metric: **"Does this measure the customer's progress on the job, or my company's internal activity?"** Prefer the former even when it's messier.
- Treat **growth initiatives** as a test: do they deepen the core job, or just sell more stuff? The latter is surface growth.

## Anti-patterns
- Running the business off **operations dashboards** (active data) while no one owns the qualitative reality of the customer's job.
- **Line-extending into adjacencies** because "we need to grow," diluting the purpose brand and confusing customers about what job you do.
- Letting **preexisting models filter the data** so disconfirming signals never register.

## Key Takeaways
1. Growth itself spawns **three fallacies** that swap the job for the product/operations — they're structural, not personal failings.
2. **Active operations data** will always be easier than **passive job data**; deliberately fund the harder kind.
3. Resist **surface growth** and **conforming data**; keep asking what *progress* the customer is really hiring you for.
