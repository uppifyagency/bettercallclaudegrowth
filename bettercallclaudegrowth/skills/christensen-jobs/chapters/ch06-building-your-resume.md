# Chapter 6: Building Your Résumé

## Core Idea
Once you understand the job, translate it into a **job spec**, then design your product as a **résumé** that perfectly answers that spec — including the *experiences* of purchasing and using it. Experiences, not features, are what win the hire and earn a purpose brand.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Job Spec**: the innovator's blueprint. It captures, from the customer's side:
  - the **functional, social & emotional dimensions** of the progress sought;
  - the **trade-offs** the customer is willing (and unwilling) to make;
  - the **competing set** (the full résumé of candidates, including nonconsumption);
  - the **obstacles and anxieties** that must be removed.
- **The Product as Résumé**: design backward from the spec. Your product "applies for the job" — every feature, service, and touchpoint is a line on the résumé proving you can do *this* job better than any rival.
- **Experiences that match the job**: the right **experiences of purchase and use** are how you deliver the social/emotional dimensions and dissolve anxiety. Experiences are *hard to copy* — competitors can clone features overnight but not an integrated experience.
- **Purpose brand**: when you reliably nail a job, the brand becomes mentally synonymous with the job ("I'll Uber there," "milk for my Oreos," a Disney vacation). A purpose brand:
  - acts as a **two-sided signal** — tells customers you'll do the job, and guides employees on what to deliver;
  - commands **premium prices** and durable loyalty;
  - is among the most valuable assets a firm can build.

## Key Concepts
- **Removing obstacles is product design**: identifying and engineering away the barriers and anxieties (clear pricing, easy setup, reassurance, guarantees) is as important as the core function.
- **Premium prices come from the whole job**: customers pay more when you nail the functional + social + emotional job and de-risk the switch — not for raw features.
- **Experiences span the entire arc**: discovering, buying, onboarding, using, getting help, upgrading — each is a chance to deliver the job or fail it.

## Mental Models
- Think **"résumé vs. job spec"**: never design features in a vacuum; design lines of a résumé answering a specific posting.
- Treat **anxiety-reduction and obstacle-removal** as first-class features with their own line items.

## Anti-patterns
- Competing on **feature checklists** — easily copied, and they ignore the social/emotional job and the anxieties.
- Building a brand that's just a name/logo rather than a **purpose brand** tied to a job — it can't command premium or guide the organization.

## Key Takeaways
1. Convert the job into an explicit **job spec**, then build the product as a **résumé** that answers it.
2. **Experiences of purchase and use** — not features — deliver the social/emotional job and resist copying.
3. Nailing the job repeatedly creates a **purpose brand**, which earns premium prices and aligns the whole company.
