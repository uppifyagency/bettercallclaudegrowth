# Chapter 7: Integrating Around a Job

## Core Idea
Sustained competitive advantage doesn't come from products or resources — rivals copy those. It comes from the **processes** by which a company integrates to get the customer's job done. Processes are invisible, hard to imitate, and where the job actually gets delivered.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Integrate around the job, not the function**: the customer's job cuts across the org chart's functional silos. Winners design and align their processes to deliver the *whole* job seamlessly, even where that's organizationally awkward.
- **Processes as the durable moat**: resources (people, tech, cash) are visible and tradeable; *processes* — how the firm coordinates to do the job — are tacit and very hard to copy. This is why competitors can buy the same equipment and still lose.
- **"If you can't describe what you're doing as a process, you don't know what you're doing"** (Deming, via the authors): you can only deliver a job reliably once you can articulate the process that produces it.
- **Mayo Clinic**: organizes every process around the patient's whole job ("give me my life back / make me well"), integrating specialists who normally work in silos — a configuration rivals struggle to replicate.

## Key Concepts
- **Interdependence vs. modularity**: when the job requires tightly coordinated, novel integration, an integrated architecture wins; the firm that owns the interfaces owns the job.
- **What gets measured shapes the processes**: if you measure functional efficiency instead of job progress, your processes drift away from the customer's job (sets up Ch 8).
- **Processes encode the job**: a well-built process is the job spec made operational — it bakes the experiences (Ch 6) into how work flows.

## Mental Models
- Ask: **"What must integrate for this job to be done well?"** Then build the process across whatever silos that spans.
- Treat your **processes as the product** of strategy — the thing you most want competitors to *not* be able to see or copy.

## Anti-patterns
- Organizing around **functions/products** and forcing the customer to integrate the pieces themselves — fragmented experience, easy to beat.
- Copying a rival's resources or features while ignoring the **processes** that actually produce their advantage (the "stack fallacy" of assuming the parts equal the system).

## Key Takeaways
1. Competitive advantage lives in **processes integrated around the job**, not in resources or features.
2. The customer's job **ignores your org chart** — build cross-silo processes to deliver the whole job.
3. You can only deliver a job reliably once you can **describe it as a process** — and that process is your hardest-to-copy asset.
