# Chapter 4: Job Hunting

## Core Idea
You don't need huge budgets or big data to find rich jobs — you need to know where to look. Jobs are hiding in everyday struggles, and there are specific, repeatable places to hunt for them.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Five places to find a job to be done**:
  1. **A job close to home** — your own life. Founders who feel the struggle personally (Sheila Marcelo → Care.com; the author's own purchases) have privileged access to a real job. Intuit's Scott Cook watched his wife struggle with the checkbook → Quicken.
  2. **Competing against nonconsumption** — where people abstain entirely. The job exists but no candidate is acceptable (too costly, complex, inaccessible). Huge, invisible markets live here.
  3. **Workarounds & compensating behaviors** — when people cobble together a hack, jury-rig a fix, or contort their behavior, they're signaling a job with no good solution. (e.g., people taping, stacking, improvising.) Watch what they *do*, not what they say.
  4. **Negative jobs** — what people emphatically *don't* want to do (sit in a waiting room, fill a form, go to the doctor). "I don't want to ___" is a goldmine; remove the negative and you win.
  5. **Unusual uses** — when customers use your product in a way you never intended (baking soda in the fridge, not for baking), they're revealing a different job you can build a business around.

## Key Concepts
- **"Fracking" for jobs**: like extracting hard-to-reach energy, the richest jobs require pressure and the right technique applied to overlooked seams of everyday life.
- **Watch behavior over stated preference**: people are unreliable narrators of their own jobs; their workarounds and compensating behaviors are honest.
- **The emotional/social dimensions surface here**: negative jobs and workarounds are usually loaded with anxiety, embarrassment, or social signaling.

## Mental Models
- Ask: "Where are people **putting up with** something?" Resignation marks an unmet job.
- Ask: "What are people **hacking together**?" A workaround is a feature request written in behavior.
- Treat **"I would never…"** as a map to a negative job worth eliminating.

## Anti-patterns
- Relying only on what customers *say* in surveys/focus groups — they describe products, not jobs, and rationalize after the fact.
- Ignoring "off-label" uses of your product because they're not in the plan — you may be sitting on the real job.

## Key Takeaways
1. Jobs are findable without big budgets — start with **your own life** and the lives around you.
2. **Nonconsumption, workarounds, negative jobs, and unusual uses** are the four richest external hunting grounds.
3. Behavior beats stated preference; design your research to *observe the struggle*, not collect opinions.
