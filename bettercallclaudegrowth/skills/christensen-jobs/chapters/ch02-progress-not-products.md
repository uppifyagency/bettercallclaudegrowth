# Chapter 2: Progress, Not Products

## Core Idea
A precise definition of "job" is what makes the theory usable. A job is **the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance** — and that definition, not a loose synonym for "need," is what gives the lens its predictive power.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Job (formal definition)**: *the progress that a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance.*
  - **Progress** = movement toward a goal/aspiration — always a verb-driven struggle, never a category.
  - **Circumstance** = the situation that defines the job. Change the circumstance and you change the job, even for the same person. The circumstance — not the customer — is the unit of innovation.
  - **Functional, Social & Emotional dimensions**: every job has all three; the social ("how I'm seen") and emotional ("how I feel") often dominate the functional. Solve only the functional and you lose to whoever solves all three.
- **What a job is NOT**:
  - **Not a "need"** — needs are ever-present and too generic ("I need to eat") to direct innovation. A job is specific and situated.
  - **Not defined by customer attributes** — demographics, psychographics, "the average customer" are correlations, not causes.
  - **Not a one-time event** — jobs recur; you can map a customer's life as a stream of jobs.

## Key Concepts
- **The quality of a job definition**: too narrow ("a chocolate shake") and you can't see the competitive set; too broad ("nourishment") and it can't guide design. Aim for the level where you can see what's truly hired and fired.
- **The competitive set widens**: once framed as a job, competitors include products from other categories *and* nonconsumption — the customer's "good enough" status quo.
- **Emotional/social heft**: the man buying a fancy car may be hiring it for status and self-image far more than for transport.

## Mental Models
- A job is **a struggle in context**, so write it as a sentence with a verb and a situation: "When ___ [circumstance], help me ___ [progress], so I can ___ [deeper aim]."
- Think **progress over time**, not transaction: people don't "want a product," they want to become a better version of themselves.

## Anti-patterns
- Equating "job to be done" with "need," "use case," or "task" — it flattens the circumstance and the social/emotional dimensions, and the theory loses its edge.
- Anchoring innovation on *who* the customer is rather than *what circumstance* they're in.

## Key Takeaways
1. The exact words matter: **progress + circumstance + functional/social/emotional**. Drop any of them and the lens blurs.
2. **The circumstance is central** — it, not the customer, is the fundamental unit of innovation work.
3. A well-specified job is stable and durable; it's the firm bedrock you design against while products and technologies churn.
