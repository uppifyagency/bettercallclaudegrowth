# Chapter 10: Final Observations About the Theory of Jobs

## Core Idea
Jobs Theory is a *theory* — a statement of what causes what — built inductively from anomalies and refined at its boundaries. Understanding how the theory was built (and where it applies) is what lets you wield it rigorously rather than as a slogan.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **How good theory is built** (the three-stage cycle): (1) **describe** the phenomenon; (2) **classify** it into categories/constructs; (3) state a **causal theory** — "X causes Y *because*…". Theory improves when an **anomaly** (something it can't explain) forces better categories. Jobs Theory matured this way.
- **Constructs**: the building-block ideas (job, circumstance, progress, forces, Big/Little Hire) that make the causal mechanism usable.
- **Causal, not correlational**: the test of a good theory is that it explains *and predicts* — it tells you what to do in a *new* circumstance, which correlations never can.
- **The boundaries of the theory** (where it does/doesn't apply): a job is best expressed in **verbs and nouns** ("help me *become* confident"), *not* adjectives and adverbs (which describe *experiences*, not the job). And it must be at the **right level of abstraction** — broad enough to see the true competitive set, narrow enough to guide design.

## Key Concepts
- **Anomalies are gifts**: the data a theory *can't* explain is what improves it. Seek disconfirming cases.
- **Right level of abstraction (again)**: too abstract → can't act; too concrete → blind to competition and to the social/emotional job. Calibrate deliberately.
- **Breadth of application**: the same lens applies far beyond products — to **education, health care, careers, relationships, and personal life** (the authors apply "What job am I hiring this for?" to life choices).

## Mental Models
- Treat your understanding of any market as a **theory to be falsified**: hunt the anomaly, then upgrade your categories.
- Express jobs as **verbs + nouns** and sanity-check the **abstraction level** before trusting your definition.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating "jobs to be done" as a **buzzword/checklist** rather than a causal theory you can test and refine.
- Defining a job with **adjectives/adverbs** (describing a nice experience) and mistaking that for the job itself.
- Picking an abstraction level that **flatters your current product** instead of revealing the real competition.

## Key Takeaways
1. Jobs Theory is a **causal theory**, built by describing → classifying → explaining, and sharpened by anomalies.
2. Mind the **boundaries**: jobs are **verbs/nouns** at the **right level of abstraction**; experiences (adjectives) are how you do the job, not the job.
3. The lens is **broad** — apply "what job am I hiring this for?" to organizations *and* your own life.
