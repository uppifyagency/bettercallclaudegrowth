# Chapter 1: What is Performance Marketing

## Core Idea
Performance marketing means spending paid-ad dollars only against a measurable return — you track cost-per-action and Return on Ad Spend obsessively, then tune targeting and settings until the cost-per-conversion is low enough that the revenue model actually works.

## Key Concepts
- **Performance marketing**: Running paid ads where every dollar is justified by a measurable result (a registration, subscriber, or sale) rather than impressions or brand vanity.
- **Return on Ad Spend (ROAS)**: The revenue generated per dollar of ad spend; if you can't generate ROAS, you can't keep running ads — it is the survival gate for paid acquisition.
- **Cost per website registration**: The amount paid to acquire one sign-up; the author's case study moved this from $94 → $37 (lazy agency) → $0.74 (author).
- **Conversion that maps to revenue**: A registration is not a paid subscriber — a cheap top-of-funnel action is worthless if it doesn't convert to a revenue-generating customer, so optimize toward revenue, not just sign-ups.
- **Interest targeting**: Choosing audiences by interests inside Meta Ads; the baseline lever that dropped cost from $94 to $37.
- **Interest layering**: Stacking/combining interests to reach a more targeted audience that converts to revenue at higher quality, not just lower cost.
- **Growth Hacking**: The low/no-budget acquisition craft the author learned before having ad budget; the precursor discipline to paid performance marketing.
- **Engagement rate after sign-up**: A post-conversion quality signal — the author's winning campaign produced both cheaper registrations and higher post-signup engagement than all other channels.
- **"Stupid tax"**: The money lost paying for lessons through trial and error while learning which settings and audiences work.

## Mental Models
- **Think of a registration as a means, not an end**: A $37 registration that never becomes a paid subscriber is worse than a more expensive one that does — judge cost-per-action against downstream revenue, not in isolation.
- **Use ROAS as the kill switch**: When you're bootstrapped and watching every penny, let "does this generate a Return on Ad Spend?" decide whether a campaign lives or dies.
- **Prefer layered, creative targeting over lazy defaults**: The fired agency got to $37 with minimal, uncreative interest targeting; deeper targeting + creative is what unlocks order-of-magnitude gains (toward sub-$1 registrations).
- **Treat early lessons as paid R&D**: Expect to pay "stupid tax" learning which settings help vs. hurt — the goal is to internalize what converts per product/service/app type.

## Anti-patterns
- **Optimizing for the cheapest top-funnel action instead of revenue**: Driving down cost per registration while ignoring whether sign-ups become paying subscribers leaves the revenue model broken.
- **Lazy, uncreative targeting**: Relying on minimal interest targeting with no layering or creativity (the agency approach) caps results far above what's achievable.
- **Filler over actionable tactics**: The author explicitly rejects fluff and non-actionable advice — favor concrete settings, audiences, and tools that move conversion.

## Key Takeaways
1. Define the conversion that actually maps to revenue (paid subscriber), then measure cost-per-action against it — not against vanity sign-ups.
2. Use ROAS as the go/no-go gate for every campaign; if it can't pay for itself, it shouldn't run.
3. Move beyond default targeting: layer interests to reach higher-quality, revenue-converting audiences, not just cheaper clicks.
4. Expect to pay "stupid tax" early — systematically learn which ad settings and audiences improve vs. hurt results for your specific product type.
5. Order-of-magnitude improvement is real: disciplined targeting and creative took a registration from $94 to $0.74 in the author's case study.
