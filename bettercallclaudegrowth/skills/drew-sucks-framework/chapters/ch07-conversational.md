# Chapter 7: C — Be Conversational

## Core Idea
"The point of content is connection." Something weird happens at the keyboard — "we write on robots so we sound like them." Connection comes from speaking *with* people, not making speeches *at* them.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Write to your best friend (or your Gran)**: pick one familiar person and write the whole piece as if talking to them.
  - When to use: every piece — especially to develop your voice/style early on.
  - How: imagine the specific person; let the words come out the way you'd actually say them. "You won't nail it at first, but writing to a familiar person will help you develop your voice."
- **The "Would I say this in person?" check**: a pre-publish voice filter.
  - When to use: before hitting publish.
  - How: read each line and ask "Would I say this in person?" If not, "rewrite it until it sounds like you."

## Key Concepts
- **Voice/style**: your natural way of speaking, transferred to the page — developed by writing to a familiar person.
- **Connection over performance**: speaking with > speaking at.
- **The secret "one word"**: Drew teases a single word to "slather on your writing like thick cream on a good scone" — deliberately withheld as an **open loop** (see Ch 6); his point is the technique of the loop, not the word.

## Mental Models
- The keyboard makes us **robotic by default** — actively counter it by picturing a real person.
- If you wouldn't **say it out loud to a friend**, don't publish it.

## Anti-patterns
- **Corporate/robotic tone**: formal "speeches" that kill connection.
- **Writing to an abstract audience**: produces stiff, voiceless prose.

## Key Takeaways
1. Write to one familiar person (best friend / Gran).
2. Before publishing, ask "Would I say this in person?" — rewrite until it sounds like you.
3. Conversational writing builds your voice and your reader relationships.
