# Chapter 8: K — Keep It Simple (Stupid)

## Core Idea
"Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication" (Da Vinci). With endless online noise, the simplest writer wins. Simple ≠ shallow — it means you explain your stuff the best (see Paul Graham, Richard Feynman).

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Two Simplicity Rules**:
  1. Never use a big word when you can use a small one.
  2. Never use two words when you can use one.
  - When to use: every edit pass.
- **The 3 Editing Tips**:
  1. **Use Hemingway** (the app): never publish anything over **grade 4** reading level.
  2. **Change your screen, change your state**: if you write on your laptop, edit on your phone; if you write in the morning, edit after the gym. A different context surfaces different flaws.
  3. **The 1/3rd Rule**: before publishing anything, **delete a third of it** — forces a higher word:wisdom ratio.
  - When to use: the editing phase (after drafting; ideally 24h later — see Ch 5).
  - How: run all three in sequence.

## Key Concepts
- **Word:wisdom ratio**: value delivered per word — the 1/3 cut raises it.
- **Grade 4 ceiling**: a concrete, testable readability target (Hemingway app).
- **Simple ≠ braindead**: depth explained clearly, not depth removed.

## Mental Models
- "**First drafts always suck**" — drafting and tidying are different jobs; you can only tidy thoughts once they're on paper.
- The **simplest writer wins** the attention war.

## Anti-patterns
- **Mistaking simple for shallow**: stripping depth instead of clarifying it ("like 90% of content").
- **Skipping the edit**: publishing the bloated first draft.

## Key Takeaways
1. Small words over big; one word over two.
2. Edit in Hemingway; keep it at grade 4 or below.
3. Change screen/state between writing and editing.
4. Apply the 1/3rd Rule — cut a third before publishing.

## Connects To
- **Ch 5 (Clarity)**: simplicity and clarity both fight uncertainty and noise.
- **Ch 9 (Structure)**: cutting and structuring both make writing skimmable.
