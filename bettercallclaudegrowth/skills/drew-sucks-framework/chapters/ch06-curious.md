# Chapter 6: C — Curiosity Is Key

## Core Idea
The brain's hunger and curiosity centers sit side by side — "we can't help but pursue what fascinates us." Make readers curious and they keep reading; lose their curiosity and they leave.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 4 Curiosity Triggers** — four simple ways to make people curious:
  1. **Talk about what they want** — this is why you must niche down; people are intrigued by the writer giving them what they want.
  2. **Talk about what they didn't know** — make them go "huh, I did not know that."
  3. **Open loops** — tease something and resolve it later (Drew demos this by withholding his "one word" editing trick).
  4. **Tell stories** — "the best storyteller wins."
  - When to use: hooks, openings, transitions, anywhere attention might drop.
  - How: combine triggers; lead with what they want, sprinkle surprises, open loops between sections.
- **The Surprise Document**: a running file where you "save everything that catches your eye" to fuel the "didn't-know" trigger.
  - When to use: ongoing — build the asset before you need it.

## Key Concepts
- **Open loop**: an unresolved question/promise that pulls the reader forward until you close it.
- **Niche down**: narrowing the topic so it reliably hits what your audience wants.
- **Story hardwiring**: "our ancestors who ignored stories about lions behind bushes became dinner" — we're built to pay attention to stories.

## Mental Models
- Curiosity is **biological hunger** — treat unanswered questions as an appetite you create then feed.
- The **best storyteller wins**, full stop.

## Anti-patterns
- **Writing off-niche**: content nobody wanted ("if this guide were about MyLittlePony, you wouldn't still be reading").
- **Closing every loop immediately**: no forward pull, no reason to keep reading.

## Key Takeaways
1. Use the 4 triggers: what they want, what they didn't know, open loops, stories.
2. Keep a Surprise Document to stockpile "did-not-know" material.
3. Niche down so you reliably serve what readers want.
4. Open loops carry the reader to the end — where the buying happens.
