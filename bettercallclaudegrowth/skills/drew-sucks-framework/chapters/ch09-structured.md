# Chapter 9: S — Structure Is Sexy

## Core Idea
"You gotta look good to get read." The brain conserves energy; when writing looks dense, readers "move on to easier pastures." Structure makes writing skimmable so people reach the end — where the buying happens.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Skimmability Toolkit**: format for the skim that happens *before* the read. Use:
  - **Bullets**
  - **White space**
  - **Short paragraphs** (even one-sentence / one-word paragraphs)
  - **Headings and subheadings**
  - When to use: every piece, applied during formatting/editing.
  - How: break dense blocks apart; let the page breathe.
- **The Slippery Slope**: structure that pulls the eye downward so readers finish — "the magic happens at the end. You gotta get people to finish if you want them to buy."
- **Vary Sentence Length** ("we hear what we read"): the fastest structure hack.
  - How: "Sometimes use short. Sometimes use long." Read everything **out loud** before publishing; for serious editing, **record yourself reading** ("it's torture but it works").

## Key Concepts
- **Skim-then-read**: readers scan before committing; structure wins or loses them there.
- **Slippery slope**: each element makes the next easy to start, carrying readers to the CTA.
- **Auditory reading**: "we don't see what we read, we hear it" — rhythm matters as much as layout.

## Mental Models
- The brain is a **calorie miser** — dense text reads as "expensive," so it's skipped.
- Writing is **heard, not seen** — edit with your ears (read aloud / record).

## Anti-patterns
- **Walls of text**: dense blocks that signal effort and get abandoned.
- **Monotone rhythm**: uniform sentence length that sounds flat when heard.

## Key Takeaways
1. Format for the skim: bullets, white space, short paragraphs, headings.
2. Build a slippery slope so readers reach the end (and the offer).
3. Vary sentence length; read out loud (or record yourself) before publishing.
