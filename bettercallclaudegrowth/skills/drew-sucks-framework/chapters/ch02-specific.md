# Chapter 2: S — Specificity Is the Secret

## Core Idea
Vague writing paints a blurry picture; blurry makes readers uncertain, and "uncertain people are like deers in headlights — they don't act." Specificity creates clarity, authority, and appeal.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Rule of One**: before any piece, set the scene by picking exactly one of each:
  - One **True Fan** (your single ideal reader)
  - One **problem**
  - One **solution**
  - One **story**
  - When to use: at the start of every piece, before drafting.
  - How: resist the urge to add more. "If you write to everyone, you write to no one." People are busy — write like it.
- **Quantify**: replace vague claims with concrete, non-round numbers (unless round is the truth).
  - When to use: any claim, result, or proof point.
  - How: "I help people lose weight" → "I've helped **56 clients** lose an average of **21lbs** in under **4 months**." Specific numbers read as a "shut up and take my money" statement.

## Key Concepts
- **One True Fan**: the single person you write each piece for.
- **Authority through specificity**: precise numbers signal you actually did the thing.
- **Round-number tax**: round numbers feel invented; oddly specific numbers feel real.

## Mental Models
- Writing is **painting a picture in the reader's mind** — the vaguer you are, the blurrier the image.
- Unspecific writers feel like **"shady salesmen you wouldn't let in your gran's house"** — nobody believes them, let alone pays them.

## Anti-patterns
- **Writing to everyone**: dilutes the message until it lands with no one.
- **Round, vague claims**: "I help people get results" — unbelievable and unsellable.

## Key Takeaways
1. Pick One Fan, One problem, One solution, One story per piece.
2. Quantify everything you can with specific (non-round) numbers.
3. Specificity = clarity + authority + appeal; vagueness = inaction.
