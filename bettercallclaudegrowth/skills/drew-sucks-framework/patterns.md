# Patterns & Techniques — The SUCKS Framework

Concrete, reusable techniques from the e-book, ready to apply to a draft.

## The Rule of One
**When to use**: at the start of every piece, before drafting.
**How**: pick exactly one of each — One True Fan, One problem, One solution, One story. Resist adding more.
**Trade-offs**: feels narrow, but "write to everyone = write to no one." Narrow focus → sharper, more persuasive piece.

## Quantify
**When to use**: any claim, result, or proof point.
**How**: replace vague/round statements with specific numbers ("56 clients, 21lbs, under 4 months"). Avoid round numbers unless they're the literal truth.
**Trade-offs**: requires you to actually track results; pays back in instant believability.

## How To's → How I's
**When to use**: turning generic advice into relatable, fan-building content.
**How**: (1) rewrite "How to X" as "How I X"; (2) open every piece with a tidbit of your story. Reader stays the hero; you drop breadcrumbs.
**Trade-offs**: risks diary-mode if overdone — keep the reader, not you, as the hero.

## Build a Unique Mechanism
**When to use**: when you want to be memorable and un-copyable.
**How**: package an old idea into your own named framework (like SUCKS). "New solutions to old problems."
**Trade-offs**: takes upfront thinking; creates a durable, ownable asset.

## The Persuasion Proof Stack
**When to use**: any persuasive piece; layer all three when stakes are high.
**How**: (1) Authority by Association — cite studies/stats/endorsements; (2) Your Results — show what you're doing; (3) Customer Results — testimonials, case studies. Run a proof-collection habit: save every piece of feedback.
**Trade-offs**: customer results are strongest but slowest to accumulate — start collecting now.

## The "What the hell do I mean?" Edit
**When to use**: editing phase (ideally 24h after drafting — never edit while writing).
**How**: line by line, ask "What do I mean?" then "Say that instead."
**Trade-offs**: slower edit; removes cleverness that was serving your ego, not the reader.

## The 4 Curiosity Triggers
**When to use**: hooks, openings, transitions, any attention dip.
**How**: (1) talk about what they want; (2) talk about what they didn't know; (3) open loops; (4) tell stories. Feed #2 from a Surprise Document.
**Trade-offs**: open loops must eventually close, or you break trust.

## Write to Your Gran / Best Friend
**When to use**: every piece; especially while developing voice.
**How**: picture one familiar person and write as if talking to them. Pre-publish, ask "Would I say this in person?" — rewrite until it sounds like you.
**Trade-offs**: may feel too casual for stiff corporate contexts; that's usually the point.

## The 3 Editing Tips (Keep It Simple)
**When to use**: editing phase.
**How**: (1) Hemingway app, never above grade 4; (2) change screen/state between writing and editing (laptop→phone, morning→post-gym); (3) the 1/3rd Rule — delete a third before publishing. Plus: small words over big, one word over two.
**Trade-offs**: cutting a third hurts; it reliably raises quality.

## The Skimmability Toolkit + Slippery Slope
**When to use**: formatting every piece.
**How**: bullets, white space, short paragraphs (even one word), headings/subheadings — build a slope that carries readers to the end (and the CTA).
**Trade-offs**: over-fragmenting can feel choppy; balance with rhythm.

## Vary Sentence Length (Edit With Your Ears)
**When to use**: final pre-publish pass.
**How**: alternate short and long sentences. Read aloud; for serious edits, record yourself reading. "We hear what we read."
**Trade-offs**: time-consuming and awkward — but catches what the eye misses.
