# Chapter 2: Building Your Home

## Core Idea
Before writing a single word, define the unwritten contract with your audience (your core values and non-negotiables), then stand up a "good enough" tech stack fast and decide free vs. paid based on how acutely your content solves a problem. Don't overthink tools or design—the words are what matter.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Unwritten Contract**: an implicit agreement with readers covering frequency, content type, and trust (no spam, no bait-and-switch). It exists whether or not you define it; define it first to make decisions easily and without regret.
  - When to use: at the very start, before publishing, and any time you consider pivoting format/topic.
  - How: identify your non-negotiables—things that, if violated, would end the relationship. These usually tie directly to your original value prop (the YouTuber who switched from haunted videos to travel vlogs broke the contract and never recovered her audience).
- **Core Values Exercise (#8)**: surface everything you want your newsletter to be/do with no constraints, flag conflicts, reorder by importance, circle non-negotiables, resolve conflicts among them, then set guardrails/policies.
  - When to use: when starting or re-evaluating a publication.
- **Picking a Platform / Newsletter Stack**: six components needed—domain, hosting, blogging platform, ESP, modal software, analytics.
  - How: choose "good enough" and move on. Recommended: Namecheap/Porkbun for domains (avoid GoDaddy); Ghost or WordPress.org for blogging (avoid Squarespace/Wix/Medium as primary home); any solid ESP that fits your budget. Don't spend more than a few days choosing.
- **Paid vs. Free decision**: a fundamental trajectory choice (not just preference) hinging on "How much is my solution worth to someone?"
  - How: weigh paid pros (alignment, accountability, instant validation, monetizes niche audiences) against cons (lost distribution vectors, lower ceiling, platform walls, must be a painkiller). Free = betting on yourself like a VC, trading profit today for outsized profit later.

## Key Concepts
- **Non-negotiables**: the few things that, if messed with, void the contract with your audience; usually tied to your original value prop.
- **Create Value > Capture Value**: share openly and freely; if you create value, you'll find a way to capture it later when the time is right.
- **Type 2 decision (reversible)**: tool/ESP choices are reversible, so act with speed; switching ESPs is far easier than migrating a codebase.
- **A name "may make you, but will not break you"**: a domain can contribute to success but rarely causes failure—don't overthink or overspend.
- **Painkiller vs. Vitamin**: people can price and pay to remove a pain (headache) but rarely perceive the value of a vitamin; paid content must be a painkiller.
- **Best of Both Worlds**: keep a free list for growth and ungate selectively; free-to-paid conversion typically runs 3–10%.
- **Bedrock / evergreen content**: durable content that drives ongoing traffic, freeing you from being a slave to a publishing schedule.
- **Threshold of excellence**: a quality bar (Steph's: never below 9/10) below which nothing gets published.

## Mental Models
- Use "Don't compare your year 0 to someone's year 10" when your site feels inferior—successful sites started ugly too (Tim Ferriss' 2006 blog).
- Think of paid vs. free like raising venture capital vs. bootstrapping: a free newsletter trades profit today for a bigger reachable ceiling tomorrow.
- Think of publishing speed like driving: ask "What's the fastest route that makes it unlikely I crash?"—maximize worthwhile "at bats" while training to play 20 seasons.
- Use "no one cares what stack you're using" when tempted to tool-shop—readers only care that it works (Ryan Holiday pasted emails into Gmail for a year).

## Anti-patterns
- **Tool/design obsession at the start**: spending weeks picking platforms or perfecting design delays the only thing that matters—creating; you can't pick the "best" tool because needs change.
- **Cheap hosts (GoDaddy, BlueHost, HostGator)**: enticing pricing but painful and risky to switch off later.
- **Giving paid subscribers "more of the same"**: subscription fatigue is real; more content isn't the value—a distinct, more-acute solution is.
- **Pricing by your time/effort instead of perceived value**: trying to change perceived value is hard; price by what the solution is worth to the reader.
- **Gating your best content**: your best thinking stuck behind a paywall can't be shared or rank on SEO, slowing growth.

## Key Takeaways
1. Define your non-negotiables now or someone will write the contract for you.
2. Select something good enough and focus on creating—stack and design don't matter to readers.
3. Treat tooling as reversible (Type 2); act with speed and switch later if needed.
4. If you go paid, your value proposition must be crystal clear and your content a painkiller, not a vitamin.
5. Pre-launch paid needs a distribution vector; otherwise start free and convert later (expect 3–10%).
6. Build a writing habit to put the reps in—write consistently about what you know; quality trumps speed.
7. Consistency matters less than staying above your threshold of excellence, especially once you have bedrock content.
