# Chapter 6: Extras — Mistakes & Good Problems

## Core Idea
Steph Smith closes by sharing the personal missteps she made building and scaling publications, plus the three "good problems" that surface once your content actually takes off. The throughline: protect your attention, default to action, and never lose the edge or the "why" that got you here.

## Biggest Mistakes (Anti-patterns)
- **"If You Write It, They Will Come"**: The belief that great content alone is enough — that if your work is wonderful, people will find it. The world does not work like that. The fix: stop spending 90%+ of your time on writing; balance it more proportionally with distribution, even if publishing cadence must suffer. Test different channels consistently until a few really work for you, then consistently invest in bedrock channels so you build a machine that runs while you sleep.
- **Getting Too Micro**: Obsessing over small optimizations (button colours, exact email send times, rigid consistency) and watching Google Analytics "like a fireworks show." There's no conclusive best send-time, and compulsively tracking unsubscribes isn't healthy. The fix: make sure the system runs smoothly, then focus on growth, not perfecting retention. Don't hyperfocus on short-term fluctuations (good or bad) — focus your time on the things that move the metrics that matter most. Keep your eyes on the horizon.
- **Not Defaulting to Action**: When overwhelmed by information, humans over-complicate and reflexively plan instead of act. Over-planning a whole project (e.g., mapping every step of interviewing 12 experts) turns it into a weight instead of "a bicycle that you're just continuing to peddle," and most people stall in pure overwhelm. The fix: for reversible decisions, build a habit of taking action — look only a few steps ahead and get the ball moving (send 5 emails, not a full plan).

## Good Problems to Have
- **Losing Your Time**: As your entity grows, more people reach out, often with requests. Still respond and engage, but learn to say no — add a filter that screens for people serious about (and respectful of) your request: ask for questions beforehand, charge for your time (Patreon, booking page), and "charge before you're comfortable doing so." Also don't be afraid to hire help; your time is your most valuable asset.
- **Losing Your Edge**: Great blogs win because they're better than alternatives (funnier, higher quality, more deeply researched, more opinionated). The wear and tear of unsubscribes, Reddit pile-ons, and articles that underperform makes you question things and write with less conviction — abandoning the very approach that got you there. The fix: remember you earned your way in front of that 15k audience; don't dilute your voice on the ascent up the mountain.
- **Losing Your "Why"**: Creators eventually forget why they started — sharing an opinion, feeling important, helping others, or just enjoying a hobby. Don't get so lost chasing subscribers, pageviews, and constantly one-upping yourself that you forget why you opted in to begin with. Follow your compass.

## Key Concepts
- **Type 1 decisions (Jeff Bezos)**: High-consequence, irreversible decisions that justify heavy planning — unlike reversible decisions, where action should be the default.
- **Bedrock channels**: Durable distribution channels (Google/SEO, Quora answers, your newsletter) worth consistent investment so growth compounds on autopilot.
- **100 true fans**: The reminder of what got you there — writing for your 100 true fans (a riff on Kevin Kelly's 1,000 True Fans).

## Mental Models
- "You only have to see 200 feet ahead of you in order to go 3500 miles" (Sam Parr) — default to action by illuminating just the next few steps.
- Knowing where you want to get is a powerful force, even if you don't know exactly how you'll get there.
- People overestimate what they can do in a day (and a year) and underestimate what they can do in ten years (Bill Gates) — so take at least one step forward every single day.
- Ask each morning: "What can I do today that will help me get closer to my goals?"

## Key Takeaways
1. Great content is not enough — balance creation with distribution and invest in a few bedrock channels.
2. Don't get too micro: ignore short-term fluctuations and focus only on metrics that matter.
3. Default to action on reversible decisions — look a few steps ahead, not the whole road.
4. Protect your time: respond but say no, filter requests by charging, and hire help.
5. Don't lose your edge — keep writing with the conviction that earned your audience.
6. Don't lose your "why" — follow your compass instead of chasing vanity metrics.
