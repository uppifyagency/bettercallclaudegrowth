# Chapter 3: Distribution (non-SEO)

## Core Idea
No matter how good content is, if you don't invest in getting it in front of people, no one will see it — the writing-to-distribution ratio should be ~50:50. Distribution is a multiplier on top of quality (base), and you must intentionally design your own distribution system across diversified channels rather than wait for a viral hit.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Distribution is a Multiplier**: quality of the article is the base, distribution is the multiplier — low quality (1) x bad distribution (1) = 1, but high quality (10) x good distribution (50) = 500. A great unshared article and a bad over-shared article both fail.
  - When to use: deciding whether to write more or promote more.
  - How: bring articles into the top 1% (returns are non-linear — better to write 10 top-1% articles than infinite average ones), then match distribution investment to creation investment.
- **Brick by Brick (6 channel types)**: classifies all traffic into Bedrock (evergreen/foundation — SEO, newsletter, Quora), Viral (Hacker News, Reddit, Pocket), Social (Twitter, LinkedIn, Facebook), Syndicate (Medium, YouTube, Pinterest), Targeted (niche communities, partnerships), and Paid.
  - When to use: building a balanced channel portfolio. Mountain analogy: bedrock = building your own elevator; viral = lottery for a helicopter ride; social = a lottery that improves over time; syndicate = building a different mountain; targeted = borrowing someone's helicopter; paid = paying people to build your elevator.
  - How: bedrock channels are the biggest missed opportunity — start there; channels support each other in tandem.
- **The CODES Framework**: evaluate each channel across Cost, Ownership, Dependability, Effort, Scalability.
  - When to use: prioritizing/balancing channels given your time and budget constraints.
  - How: scalability is at odds with ownership — keep one foundational owned platform (newsletter) plus scalable platforms you don't own; don't put all eggs in a no-ownership basket.
- **Explore and Exploit**: select 1-3 channels to explore in 2-4 week cohorts, cut channels without product-channel fit, double down (exploit) on what works, until you have 2-5 reliable channels; then tier each: Tier 0 (no-brainers: SEO, referral program, newsletter), Tier 1 (work uniquely for you, every piece), Tier 2 (certain pieces only), Tier 3 (currently testing).
  - When to use: deciding what to test and where to focus. Focus beats "spray and pray" — you need focus to get over each channel's initial hump.
- **Informational vs Inspirational articles**: informational answers a question people already search ("how to delete Facebook"); inspirational pieces aren't searched for but pique interest ("I Sell Onions on the Internet") and are the ones that go viral.

## Distribution Channels Covered
- **SEO** (bedrock): the most underrated and dependable way to grow consistently (covered in depth in Ch 4).
- **Quora Answers** (bedrock): answer high-intent questions your audience asks, include your link natively, sponsor answers to leapfrog Google.
- **Your Newsletter** (bedrock, most sacred): the only channel you truly own — an email subscriber is worth 100x a Twitter/LinkedIn follower (Andrew Chen); be a "better fisherman" via modals, welcome series, referrals.
- **Conversion Modals**: event-triggered, fixed, and landing-page modals — use a combination; event-triggered (exit) modals drive ~half of conversions, conversion landing pages convert highest (~20%).
- **Lead Magnets / Loss Leaders**: gate an asset behind email; tailor per-article ("content upgrades") for higher conversion.
- **Referral / Ambassador Programs** (often a no-brainer on CODES): make sharing low-friction with tiered rewards; pair with referral-gated Giveaways for quality scale.
- **Hacker News** (viral): it's a community, not just an upvoting site — keep titles matter-of-fact, only post novel pieces, use Show HN/Ask HN, build karma, never ask for upvotes (voting rings get docked).
- **Other Upvoting Sites** (Designer News, Lobsters, Product Hunt): content is king, your name is irrelevant; launch your newsletter on Product Hunt only after building a few thousand subscribers.
- **Pocket, Flipboard, Google Discover** (viral): save every article; can't reliably game them, but big one-off spikes (a Pocket newsletter feature drove ~80k sessions).
- **Influencers**: don't just share their work — remix/add unique value so they (and their followers) share you back without being asked.
- **Twitter** (social): be known for X, follow only value-adders, ask facilitating questions, use/update threads, build an audience before you need one.
- **LinkedIn** (social): works for specific professional audiences; "mass connect" with a clear target (e.g. 30-40 subs/week).
- **Facebook** (social): personal/page feeds rarely worth it — only valuable in groups or with paid behind it.
- **Medium Syndication**: republish your full article via the import tool (sets canonical to your URL), submit to Publications, opt into the Partner Program.
- **YouTube / Pinterest / Podcasts** (2nd & 3rd largest search engines): repurpose written content; master one at a time; Pinterest is the most overlooked and easiest entry.
- **Targeted Communities** (Slack, Telegram, Facebook, LinkedIn, Quora groups): rename to "Name from [Company]", add value before capturing it, track keywords (F5bot/Syften), ask members what other communities they love.
- **Partnerships / Cross-promotions**: newsletter swaps with aligned, engaged lists; build genuine relationships with curators before asking.
- **Guest Posting**: trade time for a larger audience — vet for size/audience fit; as a publisher, generally don't accept guest posts (misaligned incentives).
- **(Not So) Niche Forums** (Dev.to, Elpha, Indie Hackers, Behance, Dribbble): post milestones; building your own community can itself become a distribution vector.
- **Reddit** (viral + targeted): massive and super-critical; post full content with a small CTA, respect each subreddit's rules, use anvaka + Subreddit Stats for discovery.
- **Paid Growth**: only after knowing subscriber LTV; optimize around high-quality subscribers, not raw signups; buying newsletter ads is among the best paid channels.

## Key Concepts
- **Bedrock channels**: evergreen/foundation channels (SEO, newsletter, Quora) that take longest to build but compound and stay dependable.
- **Viral platforms**: undependable, low-effort, high-reach channels (Hacker News, Reddit) — post on them but never depend on them.
- **Weak ties**: casual acquaintances/common-background contacts who offer novel opportunities (people are 58% more likely to land a job via weak ties).
- **Build in the open**: sharing your goals and struggles ("pushing your car along the road") so people help you — trials, not just wins, build the audience relationship.
- **Things that don't scale**: linear, manual early-stage tactics (DMing contacts, standing in front of classes) to get your first ~100 fans.
- **Catchability / fisherman metaphor**: improving how well other channels' traffic converts to newsletter subscribers via better "nets" (modals, welcome series, referrals).
- **Loss leader**: a product/asset sold at a loss to acquire a customer you'll recoup on later (only when unit economics are solid).
- **Hollow metrics**: surface numbers (raw list size, follower count) that look good but hold little weight — what you measure is what you move.
- **SMART goals**: Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound — best set as inputs (articles written, people reached) so outputs follow.

## Mental Models
- Use **"create value before capturing it"** when entering any community/platform — join, listen, learn how people speak before promoting.
- Use **"do things that don't scale"** when you have under ~100 fans — reach out 1:1, respond to every comment, because exponential curves look linear at small scale.
- Use **"diversify to become antifragile"** when one channel dominates — you own nothing except your newsletter, so balance channels that weigh differently across CODES.
- Use **the rule of 7** when judging early results — buyers need ~7 brand engagements before converting, so consistency and compounding matter more than any single viral hit.

## Anti-patterns
- **Blogging to go viral**: "If you blog to go viral, you might watch your entire blog go down the hole" (Ali Mese) — viral hits aren't replicable or dependable.
- **Spray and pray across channels**: spreading 1 month each over 5 channels leaves a handful of followers everywhere; focus gets you over the hump.
- **Over-promotional posting**: viral/community platforms (HN, Reddit) are allergic to embellished, promotional content and will dismiss or ban you.
- **Generic modal copy**: "Sign up for my newsletter" labels the action instead of speaking to the reader's value/language; nobody wants more emails.
- **Conflicting CTAs on one page**: each page should drive a single action — don't mix course-buy and newsletter-subscribe prompts.
- **Optimizing the small stuff**: obsessing over send frequency, send time, length, and deliverability rarely moves the needle over a 10-year horizon.
- **Chasing hollow metrics**: a 10k list at 50% open rate beats a 30k list at 15%; biased survey data and raw subscriber counts mislead.

## Key Takeaways
1. Match your distribution investment to your creation investment (~50:50) — content is the base, distribution the multiplier.
2. Start with bedrock channels (SEO, newsletter, referral program) — they're the biggest missed opportunity and compound over time.
3. Use CODES to build a balanced portfolio anchored by one owned foundational platform (newsletter) plus scalable channels you don't own.
4. Explore in short cohorts, cut what lacks product-channel fit, then exploit and tier your winners; keep doing what works until it stops.
5. Get your first 100 fans by doing things that don't scale and leveraging weak ties and building in the open.
6. Treat every platform as a community — create value before capturing it; match copy to each platform's "vibe."
7. Set SMART goals (preferably input-based), watch leading indicators, and avoid hollow metrics — what you measure is what you move.
