# Chapter 6: Post Free Content I: The Content Unit (Hook–Retain–Reward)

## Core Idea
Posting free content is a 1-to-many warm advertising method that turns strangers into a growing warm audience by rewarding the people who consume it. Every piece of audience-growing content — short or long — is built from the same atom: a **Content Unit** made of three parts, **Hook → Retain → Reward**.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Content Unit (Hook–Retain–Reward)**: The smallest amount of material it takes to hook, retain, and reward attention. Can be as small as one image, meme, or sentence — that's why a tweet or jingle can go viral (it does all three at once).
  - When to use: Every time you make any piece of free content, on any platform or media type.
  - How: (1) **Hook** — give them a reason to redirect attention to you; (2) **Retain** — keep them consuming via curiosity; (3) **Reward** — satisfy the reason they started consuming. Link multiple units together to make longer content.

- **The 5 Topic Categories (Far Past, Recent Past, Present, Trending, Manufactured)**: Where to source hookable topics. Prefer personal experience — "there's only one of you," so it's the easiest way to differentiate.
  - When to use: At the Hook stage, choosing what your content is about.
  - How: **Far Past** = important life lessons tied to your product ("give them the story without the scar"); **Recent Past** = do stuff, then talk about it (meetings, calls, testimonials, case studies); **Present** = capture ideas the instant they arrive, post them publicly to test interest; **Trending** = apply your experience to what's hot right now; **Manufactured** = pick an interesting topic, then go create/do the experience (highest cost, biggest payoff).

- **The 7 Headline Components (Recency, Relevancy, Celebrity, Proximity, Conflict, Unusual, Ongoing)**: Timeless drivers of interest, drawn from a meta-analysis of news headlines. Include at least two per headline.
  - When to use: Writing the headline that communicates the reason to consume.
  - How: **Recency** (as new as possible); **Relevancy** (personally meaningful to them); **Celebrity** (prominent people/authorities); **Proximity** (close to home); **Conflict** (opposing ideas/people); **Unusual** (odd, rare, bizarre); **Ongoing** (still-unfolding stories with plot twists).

- **The 3 Retention Devices (Lists, Steps, Stories)**: Ways to embed unresolved questions in the mind so people want to know what happens next.
  - When to use: At the Retain stage, to hold attention.
  - How: **Lists** = themed items presented in sequence (state the count up front: "7 Ways…"), flexible but less explicit reward; **Steps** = ordered actions toward a goal, less flexible but more explicit reward; **Stories** = events real or imagined with a takeaway. Interweave freely (lists within steps, a story per item).

## Key Concepts
- **Post Free Content**: A warm, 1-to-many Core Four method — say it once, reach many — that builds a warm audience over time.
- **Warm audience**: The compounding asset. People who find your content valuable join it; people they share with join it; sharing can compound infinitely.
- **Content vs. audience**: The content isn't the compounding asset — the audience is. Individual posts disappear; the audience keeps growing.
- **Hook**: Giving someone a reason to redirect attention toward you; measured by the percentage who start consuming.
- **Retain**: Keeping attention; curiosity is the strongest driver because people want to know what happens next.
- **Reward**: Satisfying the reason they consumed; quality = how often you reward per second of consumption — think **value per second**.
- **Content unit**: The minimum material that hooks, retains, and rewards once.
- **Value per second**: The real measure of "good" content — "there is no such thing as too long, only too boring."
- **Volume works**: Audience grows in proportion to output — 10x the content grew Hormozi's audience ~10x faster.

## Mental Models
- **Think of content as a competition for attention.** You must beat every alternative the viewer has — make yourself the best option, or better-looking content hooks them before yours gets a chance.
- **Use "value per second" instead of length** when judging content. The same person bored 3 seconds into a video will binge a 900-page book — boredom, not duration, is the enemy.
- **Think of long content as linked content units.** A single step in a 5-step list is one unit; string five good ones together for a longer piece. Start small (short form) and build up, like a comedian earning a longer set.
- **Let the audience be the judge.** "No matter how good you think your content is, the audience decides." The single scoreboard: if your audience is growing, your content is good; if not, it isn't.

## Anti-patterns
- **Believing there's a secret blueprint**: "Anyone telling you there's some secret is trying to sell you something." The real lever is doing more — "Simple. Not easy."
- **Letting ego dismiss content**: Hormozi wrote off Jenner/Kattan/McGregor/The Rock as "rich parents" or "lucky" — if someone makes more, they're better at the game in some way; learn from them.
- **Breaking the promise of your hook**: Promising "7 Ways" and delivering 4, delivering 7 stale ones, or aiming at the wrong audience (single guys for "make up with your spouse") = no shares, no return viewers.
- **Mismatching format to the platform**: No matter how good the substance, content that doesn't look like what the audience expects to reward them loses the hook.
- **Confusing short vs. long form as fundamentally different**: They're the same units; the only difference is how many times you hook-retain-reward.

## Key Takeaways
1. Build the audience, not the content — the audience is the compounding asset that makes every other advertising method more effective.
2. Make every piece of content as a Content Unit: Hook (get attention) → Retain (keep it) → Reward (satisfy it).
3. Source topics from your own life across the five categories; personal experience is the one thing no competitor can copy.
4. Engineer headlines with at least two of the seven components (Recency, Relevancy, Celebrity, Proximity, Conflict, Unusual, Ongoing).
5. Retain attention by embedding unresolved questions using lists, steps, and stories.
6. Optimize for value per second and completely answer the questions you embedded — there is no too long, only too boring.
7. Start with short form, then do more; volume plus quality units equals a growing audience.
