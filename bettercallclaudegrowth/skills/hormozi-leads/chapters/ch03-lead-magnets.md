# Chapter 3: Lead Magnets: The 7-Step System

## Core Idea
A **Lead Magnet** is a complete solution to a narrow problem, offered free or cheap, that — once solved — reveals a bigger problem your core offer solves (the "salty pretzels at a bar"). It gets more engaged leads than advertising your core offer alone, for less money, because people who pay with their time now are more likely to pay with their money later.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The 7-Step Lead Magnet System**: the build sequence for any lead magnet.
  - When to use: when advertising your core offer alone isn't getting enough engaged leads, or you sell expensive stuff that people want to learn about before buying.
  - How:
    1. Figure out the problem to solve and who to solve it for (narrow + meaningful).
    2. Figure out how to solve it (pick one of the 3 types).
    3. Figure out how to deliver it (software, info, services, physical).
    4. Test what to name it (headline, image, subheadline — in that order).
    5. Make it easy to consume (every format, every device).
    6. Make it darn good ("give away the secrets, sell the implementation").
    7. Make it easy for them to tell you they want more (the CTA).

- **The Problem-Solution Cycle**: Every problem has a solution; every solution reveals a new problem. Smaller cycles nest inside larger ones.
  - When to use: in Step 1, to pick which narrow problem your lead magnet should solve.
  - How: pick a narrow, meaningful problem → solve it → confirm the *next* problem it reveals can be solved by your **core offer** (in exchange for money). If yes, you've got a winner.

- **The Three Types of Lead Magnets** (Step 2): every lead magnet solves one problem and reveals another via one of these.
  - **1) Reveal Their Problem** — think "diagnosis." Best when the problem worsens the longer they wait (speed test, posture analysis, termite inspection).
  - **2) Samples & Trials** — full but brief access to your core offer (limit uses, time, or both). Best when your core offer is a recurring solution to a recurring problem (Costco samples, free adjustment, consumables).
  - **3) One Step of a Multi-Step Process** — give one valuable step free, sell the rest. Best when your core offer solves a complex, multi-step problem (this book; first coat of garage sealant; free finance calculators).

- **Four Delivery Methods** (Step 3): software (give them a tool), information (teach them something), services (do work for free), physical products (give them something to hold). 3 types × 4 delivery methods = up to 12 lead magnets per narrow problem — make many versions and rotate them.

## Key Concepts
- **Lead Magnet**: a complete solution to a narrow problem, free/low-cost, that reveals a bigger problem solved by your core offer.
- **Core Offer**: what you sell for money; it must solve the next problem the lead magnet reveals.
- **Engaged lead**: a lead who has shown interest by consuming the lead magnet — the goal of every step here.
- **Grand Slam Offer**: an offer so good people feel stupid saying no — works for free stuff as much as (or better than) paid stuff.
- **Call To Action (CTA)**: tells the audience (1) what to do and (2) a reason to do it right now.
- **Scarcity**: limited supply (small vs. demand) makes people want it more and act now; use *real* business limits ("ethical scarcity").
- **Urgency**: a short time window makes people act faster; create it with deadlines, expiring discounts, or bonuses.
- **Fraternity Party Planner ("Make Up A Reason")**: any "because…" reason boosts action — even one that doesn't fully make sense beats no reason at all.

## Mental Models
- **Think of your lead magnet as salty pretzels at a bar**: it solves hunger (narrow problem) cheaply, which reveals thirst (bigger problem) solved by a drink (your core offer, sold for money) that nets a profit.
- **Use "give away the secrets, sell the implementation"** when you're scared of giving too much: the marketplace judges your free stuff and builds (or destroys) your reputation on it. You can never give too much value; you can give too little.
- **Pay for your share before the deal starts**: provide more value before they buy than the cost of your core offer — people buy based on expected value, and pre-purchase value is the best proof of it.
- **Don't be clever, be clear**: "call now" beats "don't delay." Five times more people read the headline than anything else (Ogilvy: "you've spent 80 cents of your dollar").

## Anti-patterns
- **Giving away "sucky fluff"**: weak free content makes prospects think you suck, so they buy from someone else AND tell others not to buy from you — a vicious cycle.
- **Solving a problem your core offer can't follow up on**: if the revealed next problem isn't something you charge to solve, the lead magnet leaks value with no payoff.
- **Leaving naming/packaging to chance**: a great lead magnet nobody notices is wasted; headline/image/subheadline drive 2x–10x engagement swings.
- **Shipping in only one format**: single-format limits take rate; multi-format gets 2-3-4x the leads for the same work ($100M Offers split ~¼ ebook / ¼ physical / ¼ audio / ¼ video).
- **Making it free when $25 of delivery cost would make it far more valuable**: a small budget lets you out-value $0 lead magnets and slash CAC.

### Lead Magnet Economics (Step 7 worked example)

| Approach | Ad cost per call | All-in per call | Close rate | Cost per customer | Return (on $10k profit) |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|-----------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Advertise core offer | $1,000 | $1,000 | 1 in 3 | $3,000 | ~3:1 |
| Advertise free lead magnet ($25 to deliver) | $75 | $100 | 1 in 10 buy | $1,000 | 10:1 |

Net: same ad budget + a lead magnet = ~3x the business; a $25 delivery budget out-values a $0 one and lowers CAC even when the magnet costs money.

## Key Takeaways
1. Try advertising your core offer first; reach for a lead magnet when leads need to know more before buying (common for expensive offers).
2. Pick a narrow, meaningful problem whose *revealed next problem* your core offer can charge to solve — that's the whole game.
3. Make the lead magnet so good you could charge for it; aim to deliver more value before purchase than the cost of your core offer.
4. Generate up to 12 versions per problem (3 types × 4 delivery methods), then rotate and test — results are often surprising.
5. Test the name relentlessly: headline first, then image, then subheadline; tiny word changes ("how to," dropping "more") swing engagement.
6. Package in every format and on every device to multiply take rate 2-4x for the same work.
7. End with a clear, simple CTA — "what to do" + "a reason to act now" (scarcity, urgency, or any "because…") — and do it often.
