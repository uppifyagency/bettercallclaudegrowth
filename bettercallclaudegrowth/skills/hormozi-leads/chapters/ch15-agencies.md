# Chapter 15: Agencies: Renting Expertise

## Core Idea
Don't hire an agency to do the work forever — hire it to teach you (and your team) a new method or platform fast, then bring it in-house. You rent their expertise on a deadline, beat their results with your own team, then drop to cheap consulting and cut them loose.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Learn-Don't-Rent (the right way to use an agency)**: The agency is a third type of **Lead Getter** (Lead Getter #3). Instead of paying forever to outsource a Core Four channel, you pay to acquire a money-making skill for life.
  - When to use: You have *some* money (good agencies cost money — broke = trial-and-error only) AND you want to learn a *new method* or *new platform* you don't yet understand.
  - How (the agreement template / 6-step playbook):
    1. Open with intent: tell them "I want to do what you do in my business... work together ~6 months so I learn how, pay extra for you to break down *why* you make each decision, then train my team, then switch to lower-cost consulting." Ask "Are you opposed to this?"
    2. Set a purpose + deadline up front (the deadline forces you and your team to actually learn).
    3. Run both teams in parallel — agency does the work *and* teaches; your team practices fulltime.
    4. Compare your team's results to the agency's every cycle until yours beats theirs.
    5. Switch to discounted consulting once you're effectively teaching them.
    6. Cut them loose; reinvest the saved money into scaling what you just learned.
  - Negotiate: most agencies say yes; if not, move to the next one. "At some price it's worth it for both of you."
- **Tiered hiring (good-enough + elite)**: For a brand-new platform, hire one "good enough" agency to learn the ropes and stay committed, then hire a more *elite* agency (Hormozi paid 4x) to learn how to *maximize* it. Best of both worlds: better short-term results (they know more now) + better long-term results (you/your team own it).
- **Pick-the-Right-Agency checklist (10 criteria)**: Use as a guide, not gospel:
  1. Someone you know got good results with them (word of mouth beats their own ads/outreach).
  2. Prominent companies you recognize got good results.
  3. They have a waiting list (demand > supply).
  4. Clear sales process that sets realistic expectations — no funny business.
  5. No short-term hacks; talk stays on long-term strategy with clear setup/scale/results timelines.
  6. They tell you exactly what they need from you, when, and how they'll use it.
  7. They propose a regular meeting schedule and multiple ways to report progress.
  8. Updates in simple terms with clear tracking so you see cost vs. results.
  9. They make a good offer — judged on the four offer levers: **Dream outcome**, **Perceived likelihood of achievement**, **Time delay**, **Effort and sacrifice**.
  10. They are expensive (all good agencies are expensive — but not all expensive ones are good).

## Key Concepts
- **Agency**: A lead-getting service business you pay to run paid ads, do outreach, or package/distribute content.
- **Lead Getter #3**: Agencies as the third leverage source for getting leads (after employees, before affiliates).
- **Ignorance tax**: The money wasted learning the wrong way; Hormozi paid it so you can skip ahead.
- **Cycle of insanity**: The agency's default model — get you excited, assign their best rep, show results, then quietly swap in a junior rep so results decay until you cancel and repeat with a new agency.
- **Consulting arrangement**: The cheaper downgrade after your team is competent — they stay on call for problems instead of doing the work.
- **Breathing room**: The deliberate overlap period where you pay both the agency *and* your team to do the same work, so the skill transfers.
- **Renting expertise**: Buying a skill faster than trial-and-error would, then owning it — vs. renting a service indefinitely.

## Mental Models
- **Think of an agency as a tutor, not a vendor.** Hormozi's origin story: he paid $750/hr to have a pro look over his shoulder while *he* ran the ads — 8 hours and ~$6,000 to learn a skill that's made millions. You're buying the *why*, not the deliverable.
- **Use the deadline as the forcing function.** Without a purpose and an explicit "by when," you fall for the lie "I'll never have to learn this because they can do it" — and become a permanent renter.
- **Your team beats the agency over time because attention is finite.** You only ever get a fraction of an agency's attention (it drops every time they sign a new client); your fulltime team only gets better. So race them.
- **"They are expensive" is a filter, not a guarantee.** Expensive is necessary but not sufficient — run the whole checklist and talk to as many as it takes.

## Anti-patterns
- **Renting forever**: Believing you'll never need to learn the skill. You end up with the agency "by the balls," paying high fees indefinitely with no in-house capability.
- **Falling for the cycle of insanity**: Accepting the senior-rep-bait then junior-rep-switch; results decay, you cancel, repeat — wasting years and money.
- **Hiring while broke**: Good agencies cost real money; with no margin to spare, skip them and learn through trial and error.
- **Picking on price alone (cheap)**: "Don't be cheap." Cheap agencies underperform; but expensive alone doesn't make them good either.
- **No overlap / no breathing room**: Trying to cut the agency before your team can reproduce results — you lose the skill transfer the whole strategy depends on.
- **Judging from their marketing**: Trusting an agency you only know via their own paid ads or cold outreach — the best ones run on word of mouth.

## Reference Tables

| Approach | Cost | Speed to results | Skill stays in-house? | Best when |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Do it yourself (trial & error) | Lowest cash, high time | Slowest | Yes | No money to spend |
| Hire agency the *wrong* way (rent forever) | High, ongoing | Fast then decays | No | Never (the trap) |
| Hire agency the *right* way (learn, then in-house) | High short-term overlap, then drops | Fast and durable | Yes | You have money + a new method/platform to master |

## Key Takeaways
1. Use agencies for exactly two things: learning new methods and learning new platforms — not for permanent outsourcing.
2. Open every agency relationship with a stated purpose, a 6-month-ish learning intent, and a deadline; pay extra to be taught the *why* behind decisions.
3. Run the agency and your own team in parallel; compare results every cycle and cut the agency once your team wins (Hormozi got this to <6 months).
4. Stack a "good enough" agency to learn the ropes with an elite agency to learn maximization for a brand-new platform.
5. Vet with the 10-point checklist — prioritize word-of-mouth reputation, a waiting list, realistic expectations, clear reporting, a strong offer (Dream outcome / Likelihood / Time delay / Effort & sacrifice), and yes, expense.
6. Budget for "breathing room": you'll pay agency + team simultaneously during transfer. Costly, but worth it when done right.
7. The better you get at this loop, the cheaper and faster it becomes each time — repeat it for every new method or platform.
