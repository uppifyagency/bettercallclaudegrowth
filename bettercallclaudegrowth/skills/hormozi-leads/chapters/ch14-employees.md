# Chapter 14: Employees: The Internal Core Four

## Core Idea
Employees are Lead Getter #2: people you train to run the same Core Four advertising you used to get your own leads. Building a business that gets leads *without you* is what converts a high-paying job into a sellable asset worth a multiple of profit.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Internal Core Four**: Recruit employees with the exact same Core Four you use to get customers. Reframe "let potential customers know about your stuff" as "let potential employees know about your stuff" — it's the same activity, just a different audience.
  - When to use: any time you need more talent to scale your advertising (more workers = more lead-getting).
  - How — the Customers → Employees mapping:
    - Warm Outreach → Asking your network
    - Cold Outreach → Recruiting
    - Post Content → Posting job openings
    - Paid Ads → Promoting job postings
    - Customer Referrals → Employee referrals
    - Affiliates → Associations, guilds, listservs
    - Agencies → Staffing firms
    - Employees → Employees (unchanged)
  - Build a *reliable, repeatable* process to get employees, just like your reliable process to get customers. You need both to scale. When you need more, do more.

- **The 3Ds (Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate)**: The training model for turning a hire into a lead-getter.
  - Document: Write the exact checklist of how you do the task. Bonus: record yourself doing it multiple ways/shifts, then watch as an observer. Test it — can YOU do an A+ job following only your own written steps?
  - Demonstrate: Do the task in front of them, walking the checklist step by step. Every time they stop or slow you to understand, fix the checklist. That's draft two.
  - Duplicate: They do it in front of you while you observe. If the checklist is right, the outcome matches. If it's off, you find out fast — fix the checklist until they nail it. Now you have a bonafide lead-getter on payroll.
  - North star: "If you vanished tomorrow, could a stranger get your results following only your checklist?"

- **Cost-per-engaged-lead from employees**: For non-paid-ads advertising (outreach, content), cost is basically payroll. Total Payroll / Total Engaged Leads = Cost per engaged lead. Chain it forward to CAC and LTGP:CAC (see Reference Tables).

- **The qualification diagnostic question**: When CAC exceeds 3x industry average, ask one question to know whether to fix advertising or sales — "Do my engaged leads have the problem I solve AND the money to spend?"

## Key Concepts

- **Lead-Getting Employee**: A person you train to get leads the exact same way you did — running ads, making/posting content, doing outreach.
- **Lead Getter (#2 = Employees)**: The second source of leverage; employees do the Core Four on your behalf so leads flow without your direct labor.
- **The time-for-management trade**: Swap ~40 hours of *doing* for ~4 hours of *managing*; repeat the trade up the stack (200 hrs doing → 20 hrs managing → 4 hrs managing a manager).
- **Asset vs. job (Scenario #1 vs. #2)**: Same revenue/profit, but a business that runs without you is a sellable asset; one that only works with you in it is a glorified job worth near-zero to a buyer.
- **Make rich vs. become wealthy**: "You get rich from what you make. You become wealthy from what you own." A self-running business can sell for a multiple of profit (e.g., $2M profit → $10M+ enterprise value).
- **Competence vs. performance**: Knowing exactly what to do (competence) is separate from being good at it yet (performance). If instructions are right but speed is low, they just need reps — "slow then smooth then fast."
- **CAC / LTGP**: Cost to acquire a customer, and lifetime gross profit; target LTGP:CAC of at least 3:1 (CAC ≤ one-third of lifetime profit).

## Mental Models

- **Think of a hire as buying back time, not buying a worse version of you.** You can't do *everything* better than *all* your employees — only some things better than each one. Replace yourself as soon as you can, then go make yourself useful where the business needs you next.
- **Use "if I can do it, someone else can do it better" as your default.** Trusting employees to succeed makes your own time and attention more valuable — if someone else can do it (or train it), why would you?
- **Treat wrong results as a checklist bug, not a person bug.** Train people to follow directions; then if they follow directions and get the wrong result, you *know* it's the directions — and you control those.
- **Diagnose before you fire.** Use the qualification question to route the problem: unqualified leads or not-enough-buyers = advertising problem; qualified-but-not-buying = sales problem. Don't fire the sales rep for an advertising problem (or vice versa).

## Anti-patterns

- **"If you want it done right, do it yourself" / "Nobody can replace me"**: False beliefs that keep you trapped doing the work. Everyone is replaceable (by people, tech, or time) — especially you. Reframe to "If you want it done right, get someone to spend all their time doing it."
- **Competing with your own employees**: Going "me against them" to prove you're more able distracts you and makes the business worse. You hired and trained them — if they suck, that's on you (no skills to train/lead, or too cheap to hire better).
- **Explaining steps to make a checklist "work"**: If you have to explain what a step means, the step is too complicated — usually multiple steps crammed into one. If they only "get it" after long explanation, the checklist needs work, not the person.
- **Punishing mistakes during training**: Penalties add pain to an already-punishing learning process. Reward the good stuff you want repeated; that's what gets repeated.
- **Multi-fault feedback**: Giving several corrections at once when someone's never done the task. One step, one piece of feedback, practice to mastery, then advance.
- **Ignoring performance dips**: A sudden drop usually means they silently stopped doing an important step. Retrain, then reward following that step going forward.

## Key Takeaways
1. To advertise more than one person can, you need more workers — train employees to run the Core Four for you.
2. Recruit talent using the Internal Core Four: map each customer-getting channel to its employee-getting twin and build a repeatable hiring process.
3. Train with the 3Ds — Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate — until a stranger could get your results from the checklist alone.
4. Hire and train anyone with work ethic and basic social skills for ground-level lead-getting; get picky only for expensive C-suite ("fancy") roles.
5. Reward direction-following over results during training; if directions are followed and results are wrong, fix the directions.
6. Measure payroll → cost-per-lead → CAC → LTGP:CAC; keep total acquisition cost at or under one-third of lifetime profit (≥3:1).
7. A business that runs without you is worth a multiple of its profit — building lead-getting employees is a net-worth decision, not just an ops decision.
