# Chapter 8: Cold Outreach: Reaching Strangers One-to-One

## Core Idea
Cold outreach is the 1-to-1, cold quadrant of the Core Four: privately contacting qualified strangers at high volume, leading with big fast value, and following up relentlessly until enough engage. It is a numbers game — once you know how much outreach engages one lead, you just do more.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Three Problems Strangers Create (and the order to solve them)**: Strangers (1) you have no way to contact, (2) ignore you even if you reach them, (3) aren't interested even if they listen. Solve in this exact order:
  - When to use: Whenever you advertise 1-to-1 to a cold audience (calls, emails, DMs, voicemails, door-knocks).
  - How: (1) **Build the list** — get a way to contact qualified strangers; (2) **Figure out what to say** — personalize + big fast value; (3) **Volume** — contact them until ready and able to listen.

- **Three Ways to Get a Targeted Lead List**: Work from most accessible to least accessible.
  - How: **Step 1 Softwares** — subscribe to scraping tools, search your exact criteria, test a few hundred per tool; if contacts are current and responsive, buy in bulk. **Step 2 Brokers** — have multiple list brokers assemble lists to your criteria, test samples, keep the winners. **Step 3 Elbow Grease** — manually scrape from groups/communities your audience lives in.
  - When to use: Default to scraping; if you can't find the right audience there, escalate to brokers, then manual. Start at Step 3 if you have more time than money.
  - Key insight: Self-assembled lists are the *freshest* (fewer competitors have hit them), but cost the most time. If you can search a database, so can everyone else.

- **What to Say = Personalize, Then Give Big Fast Value**: Two factors get strangers to engage in seconds.
  - **Personalize (Act Like You Know Them)**: Find 1-3 facts a friend would know, compliment them on it, ideally show how it benefited you. Make the cold reach out *look like a warm reach out.* People give you more time if you clearly researched them.
  - **Big Fast Value**: Give away the farm. Don't tickle interest — blow their minds in under 30 seconds. Lead with your offer, a Lead Magnet, or both. Give away stuff people *actually pay for* (not just "stuff so good they should pay"). Hormozi 3x'd take rates by swapping a "game planning session" (code for sales call) for as much free service as he could afford.

## Key Concepts
- **Cold outreach**: 1-to-1 private contact with a cold audience (strangers); the more advanced cousin of warm outreach, no longer capped by your warm audience.
- **Engaged lead**: A lead that shows enough interest to move forward; the unit cold outreach is measured in.
- **Big fast value**: The deliberate name for the lead magnet/offer — a reminder it must be BIG and FAST or you blend into the noise and get ignored.
- **Automated delivery**: Recording a message once (voicemail, video, templated email/text) so no human re-conveys it each time — unlocks scale (more engaged leads per unit time, even at lower percentage).
- **Automate distribution**: Sending at scale (robo-dialers, 1,000-email blasts) vs. dialing/clicking one at a time. You trade personalization for scale.
- **Follow up — more times, more ways**: Contact each lead repeatedly across multiple channels; then restart the whole list after 3-6 months.
- **A-C-A**: The qualify-for-a-call format carried over from warm outreach (Ch5), used once a DM/call gets a reply.
- **"Solve for X"**: The reliability equation — for every X contacts you get Y customers, so to hit a customer goal you back into the required outreach volume.

## Mental Models
- **Think of cold outreach as a numbers game**: A fixed input creates a fixed number of responses, like clockwork. Reverse-engineer desired sales to required inputs (e.g., 100 emails → 1 customer; want 100 customers → send 10,000 → 333/day → 3 people at 111/day each).
- **Use the "calling your parents" test for follow-up**: If you genuinely needed to reach someone urgently, you'd call, text, AND leave a voicemail — then do it again soon. Act like you're actually trying to reach prospects, not going through the motions.
- **Use channel-switching as a free reason to follow up**: Email first (most won't reply), then "I'm calling to follow up on my email." You either get a response or a legitimate reason to reach out again — win either way.
- **Think of re-running the list as new leads from old names**: After 3-6 months, the same list yields fresh engaged leads — because they may not have seen you, the timing was wrong, or their *circumstance* changed (e.g., gained 10 lbs over the holidays). Everything can be right except timing.
- **Match personalization to list size**: Tiny niche (1,000 hedge fund managers) → personalize every one. Huge market (tens of millions) → less personalization, more automation. The fewer leads, the less automation.

## Anti-patterns
- **Leading with the ask** ("hey man, wanna buy some marketing services?"): Gets you hung up on; personalization is what gets your foot in the door.
- **A mediocre lead magnet/offer**: You blend into the ocean of attention-seekers and get ignored just like them. Up the ante until they feel stupid saying no.
- **Contacting once and stopping**: Most people don't follow up — nobody sees 100% of what you send 100% of the time. One touch wildly under-harvests the list.
- **Expecting fast results / wrong expectations**: Hormozi planned 12 weeks; it took almost a year. Cold outreach takes high volume AND a long time. The team twice asked him to pull the plug before it scaled.
- **Not tracking metrics**: His two failed attempts hired people who never tracked; the third tracked every stat and succeeded. The operator must know the sales process numbers cold.
- **Over-polishing scripts before testing**: No awards for the prettiest script. Get your first 100 conversations / 10,000 emails out before tweaking.

## Reference Tables

### Channel benchmarks (target: ~3% of list → engaged leads; 1 customer per ~100 attempts)
| Channel | Volume/day | Funnel | Engaged leads | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phone | 100 calls | 20% pick up × 25% take magnet | ~4/day (≈1/hour over 4 hrs) | Labor-heavy |
| Email | 100 personalized | 30% open × 10% reply | ~3/day (3%) | Niche high-ticket portfolio co. hit 4%, ~30:1 ROI |
| DM / voice memo | 100 (name + 1 personal line) | 20% reply | ~20/day | Then run A-C-A to qualify |

### Cold-call unit economics (worked example)
| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Pay | $15/hr + $50 per show |
| Leads | $0.10 each, 200 called/day |
| Shows | ~2/day per rep |
| Daily cost/rep | $120 labor + $100 commissions + $20 leads = $240 → $120/show |
| Close rate | 33% of shows |
| Cost per client | $360 (ex-commissions) |
| Profit/client | $3,600 → **10:1 return** |

**Success floor:** lifetime profit per customer ≥ 3× cost to acquire (3:1 is the *bare minimum* — top operators hit 30:1).

## Key Takeaways
1. Solve the three stranger-problems in order: build the list, craft one great message, then crank volume.
2. Make cold look warm — personalize with 1-3 facts a friend would know before you ever pitch.
3. If the offer isn't working, swap the lead magnet first; give away what people actually pay for until "no" feels stupid. (3x'd Hormozi's take rate.)
4. Follow up more times in more ways, then re-run the entire list every 3-6 months for free new leads.
5. Automate delivery and distribution to scale; trade personalization for volume only as list size grows.
6. Track every stat and build the "solve for X" equation, then just add bodies — boring, tedious, brutally effective.
7. Bother because it works when most won't: no ad fatigue, invisible to competitors, reliable, few platform/compliance changes, no spokesperson needed (makes the business sellable), and hard to copy.
