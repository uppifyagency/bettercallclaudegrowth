# Chapter 9: Run Paid Ads I: Making the Ad

## Core Idea
Paid ads are 1-to-many advertising to cold audiences where you trade money for guaranteed reach — so the game is efficiency, not reach. You win by narrowing the haystack (platform, then targeting) so the right needles see an ad built from Callout + Value + CTA, then capturing their contact info to turn them into engaged leads. "Advertising is the only casino where, with enough skill, you become the house."

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The 4 Paid-Ads Problems (the chapter's spine)**: Every ad campaign solves four problems in order.
  - When to use: any time you run paid traffic to a cold audience.
  - How: (1) Find a **platform** where four things are true, (2) **Target** the right people inside it, (3) Make the best **ad** for them, (4) Get **permission** to contact them (capture lead info).

- **The 4 Platform Requirements**: Pick a platform where all four are true.
  - How: (1) You've used it and gotten value as a consumer (so you understand it), (2) you can target your people on it, (3) you know how to format ads for it, (4) you have the minimum money needed to place an ad. Platforms change; these principles don't.

- **Anatomy of an Ad = Call Out + Value + CTA**: The three core elements of every ad Hormozi makes.
  - How: (1) **Callout** to get them to notice it, (2) **Value** to give them a reason to act, (3) **CTA** to give them a way to act.

- **What-Who-When Framework (the Value section)**: How to answer "why should I be interested?" It hinges on knowing the Value Equation forwards and backwards.
  - When to use: writing the value/middle of any ad; add more angles for long ads, fewer for short ads, but keep callouts and CTAs identical.
  - How — **The What** = the 8 key elements: the 4 value drivers and their 4 opposites — Dream Outcome / Nightmare, Perceived Likelihood of Achievement / Risk, Speed / Time Delay, Ease / Effort & Sacrifice ("carrots vs sticks"). **The Who** = run each element through the people who give your customer status (spouse, kids, parents, colleagues, bosses, friends, rivals, competitors). **The When** = run each through the prospect's past, present, and future timeline. Combine all three to multiply angles.

## Key Concepts

- **Paid ads**: Renting another's audience ("renting eyeballs or earballs") to put your offer 1-to-many in front of cold strangers; fastest way to reach the most people.
- **Efficiency vs reach**: Reach is guaranteed when you pay; getting your money back isn't. The job is the push-pull ratio between how much you spend and how much they buy.
- **Lookalike audience**: A platform-built audience similar to (and bigger than) a list you upload. Feed lists in order of quality: customers → warm outreach → cold outreach to hit the minimum.
- **Targeting filters**: Layered factors (age, income, gender, interests, time, location) added on top of the lookalike to raise the percentage of right people.
- **Callout**: Whatever you do to grab attention — verbal (Labels, Yes-Questions, If-Then Statements, Ridiculous Results) or nonverbal (Contrast, Likeness, The Scene).
- **Cocktail party effect**: Even amid noise, one signal (your name) cuts through — the principle every callout harnesses.
- **CTA**: An explicit next step — "S-P-E-L-L it out" (click this button, call this number, reply YES). Can be juiced with urgency/scarcity/bonuses.
- **Click-to-close continuity**: Your landing page must match your ad's look and language so the experience never becomes a "Frankenstein."

## Mental Models

- **Needles in a haystack**: Start with the whole world, then narrow — platform cuts ~99%, language another ~0.5%, then targeting filters. Use this to remember that "right people never saw it" is the usual reason an ad loses money.
- **Puddle → pond → lake → ocean**: Prove profitability in a small specific audience first, then expand. Efficiency ratio drops as you scale, but total profit rises ($1k→$10k vs $100k→$300k). Use specificity for early wins that fund broader spend later.
- **"Sell the next second"**: Each second of an ad exists to sell the next; the headline is the first sale ("you've spent eighty cents of your advertising dollar" — Ogilvy). Put the majority of effort into the first five seconds.
- **"Quack like a duck"**: To attract a customer type, look/talk/act/dress like them and be in their environment. Likeness in spokesperson and scene out-converts even an identical message.
- **Consume ads on purpose**: Turn every ad you see into a lesson — watch how they call out, present value, and give a CTA. Become a student of the game.

## Anti-patterns

- **Targeting too broad / wrong audience**: "The right message to the wrong audience falls on deaf ears" — marketing Iowa to Florida residents fails no matter how good the ad.
- **Over-engineering the creative before the callout**: Hormozi's ugliest all-caps, no-image ad sold 19 people; effort spent past the first five seconds is misallocated.
- **No explicit CTA**: Interested prospects have huge motivation for a tiny window; if you don't tell them exactly what to do, the motivation evaporates.
- **Clunky, long domains/CTAs**: `alexsprivateequityfirm.com/free-book-and-course2782` kills clicks; make web addresses short and memorable.
- **Ad/landing-page mismatch**: Breaking the promised look and language wastes spend until you fix the continuity.
- **Never building the landing page**: Hormozi wasted four years too scared to make one, then finished it before lunch with drag-and-drop tools.

## Key Takeaways

1. Solve the four problems in order: platform → targeting → ad → permission. Don't overcomplicate it; you're just narrowing who sees the ad.
2. Build the lookalike audience from your best lists first (customers, then warm, then cold), then layer filters — more filters = more efficient but faster burn.
3. Spend most of your creative effort on the first five seconds (the callout); if they never notice it, nothing else matters.
4. Write the value section with What-Who-When: run all 8 value elements through every status-giving person and across past/present/future to generate near-infinite angles.
5. Make the benefits look as big as possible and the costs as small as possible — that's what creates an engaged lead.
6. Always end with a spelled-out, frictionless CTA, then capture contact info on a simple landing page that matches the ad.
7. You're not selling on the ad — you're asking if they're interested; when they give you contact info, they become an **engaged lead**.
