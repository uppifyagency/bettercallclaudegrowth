# Chapter 2: 3 Steps to Productizing Yourself

## Core Idea
Productizing yourself is a strict sequence: **(1) Put in the reps → (2) Discover repeating processes → (3) Solve problems at scale.** You cannot skip steps — products built without mastery and without systematized processes have nothing real to sell.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 3-Step Productization Method** — the core framework of the whole book.
  1. **Put in the reps** — hone the skill and publish, doing the unscalable work.
  2. **Discover repeating processes** — systematize/automate what you do for every client.
  3. **Solve problems at scale** — turn skill + systems into products that sell 24/7.
  - When to use: As the master roadmap for anyone escaping a service business.
  - How: Run them in order; each step is the prerequisite for the next.

- **10,000 *iterations*, not 10,000 hours** — Jack's twist on Gladwell. Mindless repetition doesn't count; what compounds is iteration with feedback.
  - When to use: Whenever you confuse "time served" with "skill gained."
  - How: Treat each rep as a deliberate attempt to be *better* than the last.

- **Get going, then get good** — start before you're ready; quality is a function of volume + feedback, found in public, not in private preparation.

- **Deliberate Practice** (Ericsson; cited via *Outliers*, *Deep Work*, *Peak*):
  - When to use: Structuring how you actually improve.
  - How: (a) set a specific goal, (b) put in purposeful (not habit-based) reps, (c) target specific weaknesses within the discipline, (d) build a system to receive and apply feedback.

- **"Lose your job"** — deliberately replace yourself with systems and tools so the business doesn't depend on your manual involvement.

## Key Concepts
- **Proof of work** — published output beats résumés; *what you write/design/build* is the credential.
- **Self-selection** — niche marketing copy makes good clients qualify themselves in and bad ones out.
- **Leverage (onboarding assets)** — forms/videos/docs that do the explaining once, forever.
- **Exponential vs. linear delivery** — products reach more people at the same (or lower) cost.

## Mental Models
- **Unscalable first, scalable later** — "Finding that first client requires the unscalable stuff: applying to 150 jobs, sending 100 sales letters, posting 100 illustrations." Earn the right to scale.
- **"It's not about never doing things that don't scale. It's about not repeating yourself when you don't have to."** (Jack Butcher)
- **Writer's block = being unprepared** — decide what you'll create in advance and the block disappears.
- **A template gives someone the framework; they fill in the specifics** — selling answers, not hours.

## Reference Tables

**Step 2 — ways to systematize repeating work:**
| Tactic | What it does |
|---|---|
| Email templates | Reuse onboarding / feedback / follow-up messages; never forget a detail |
| Process documentation | Onboards clients *and* doubles as marketing proof of professionalism |
| Vet clients with copy | Niche the offer to one thing → clients self-select |
| Use forms | Gather standard client info without a call or long email |

**Step 3 — ways to solve problems at scale:**
| Product type | Best when |
|---|---|
| Write a (short, self-published) book | Knowledge teaches a skill; no need for 200 pages or Penguin |
| Create a course | You can walk someone through solving a problem (studio *or* voiceover-on-slides) |
| Sell templates | Solving the problem = handing over the right framework to fill in |
| Automate with software | A dashboard/tool/automation can replace part of the manual service |

## Anti-patterns
- **Skipping reps or processes**: launching products before mastery → nothing real to sell.
- **Starting every project from a blank page**: re-doing research/work that prior projects already solved.
- **Mistaking repetition for practice**: doing the same task mindlessly never produces mastery.
- **Hoarding your job**: refusing to systematize because "only I can do it."

## Key Takeaways
1. The steps are ordered and non-skippable: reps → processes → scale.
2. Aim for 10,000 *iterations* with feedback, not 10,000 hours of grind.
3. Publish as you learn — proof of work makes you a magnet for clients.
4. Templatize emails, docs, vetting copy, and intake forms to reclaim time.
5. Turn the systematized skill into a book, course, template, or software that sells 24/7.
6. Build a publishing routine: calendar, plan-ahead topics, and *engage with everyone who engages with you*.
