# Chapter 6: The Thought Process: Convergent vs Divergent Thinking

## Core Idea
Before you can build a Grand Slam Offer, you must switch your brain out of school-trained convergent (one-right-answer) thinking and into divergent thinking: generating MANY ways to deliver value for a single problem. Life pays you for divergent problem-solving, and offer creation is a divergent exercise.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Convergent vs Divergent Thinking**: Two distinct modes of problem-solving you choose between deliberately.
  - **Convergent**: many known, unchanging variables that funnel down to a single, binary (right/wrong) answer. "Think math." Schools train this because it is easy to grade.
  - **Divergent**: multiple variables (known AND unknown), dynamic conditions, and multiple valid answers — with one answer that is "way more right" than the others.
  - When to use: use divergent thinking to invent and assemble offers; use convergent thinking only for closed-form arithmetic (e.g., "how many salespeople must I hire?").
  - How: take a single goal (deliver value to your customer), then list as many distinct ways to reach it as possible BEFORE judging or narrowing.

- **The Brick Exercise**: A 120-second divergent-thinking warm-up. Set a timer and write down every possible use of a brick.
  - When to use: run it (or its mental equivalent) to prime the creative mode you need for offer building.
  - How: (1) Time-box to 120 seconds. (2) Brainstorm uses without filtering. (3) Then challenge your hidden assumptions — how big is the brick? what is it made of (plastic, gold, clay, wood, metal)? what shape (holes, divots, jumbo)? (4) Notice that loosening fixed assumptions unlocks far more uses (paperweight, door stop, flotation device if plastic, payment/retainer of value if gold, plant holder if holed, resistance-training weight, a seat if jumbo).
  - Lesson: changing the attributes of the "thing" multiplies the value it can deliver — exactly how you should think about your own offer's building blocks.

## Key Concepts
- **Divergent problem-solving**: Generating many solutions to one problem; the skill "life pays you for."
- **Convergent problem-solving**: Collapsing fixed variables to one binary answer; useful but limited.
- **Multiple right answers**: Under divergent thinking, several solutions work — your job is to find the one that is dramatically more right.
- **Offer building blocks**: The discrete pieces that, when combined, make an offer irresistible.
- **Value creation via combination**: Finding the customer's desire first, then devising as many ways as possible to combine building blocks to satisfy it.
- **Assumption-loosening**: Questioning the fixed attributes (size, material, shape) of a thing to expand its possible uses/value.

## Mental Models
- **Think of your offer like a brick.** Its value isn't fixed — change its attributes and combinations and you change everything it can do. Don't accept the "standard brick" version of your offer.
- **Use divergent thinking to expand, convergent thinking to choose.** First go wide (list every way to deliver value), then narrow — never narrow first. (This sets up the Trim & Stack convergence in Ch8.)
- **Anchor on the customer's desire, not on your product.** "If I were selling a brick, I'd find out what my customer's desire was, then devise how many ways I could create value with my brick." Start from the Dream Outcome, then brainstorm delivery methods.
- **Treat hidden assumptions as adjustable variables.** When ideas dry up, ask what you've silently held constant (size, material, format, delivery) and vary it.

## Anti-patterns
- **Defaulting to convergent thinking**: Hunting for the one "correct" offer kills creativity; offers have many right answers, and the best ones come from generating volume first.
- **Judging while brainstorming**: Filtering ideas as you generate them shrinks your option set — separate the generate phase from the trim phase.
- **Accepting fixed assumptions**: Treating your product's attributes as unchangeable ("it's just a brick") blinds you to higher-value configurations.

## Key Takeaways
1. Switch into divergent mode before designing any offer — quantity of distinct value-delivery ideas comes first.
2. Identify your offer's building blocks and brainstorm as many ways to combine them as possible.
3. Start from the customer's desire (Dream Outcome), then reverse-engineer many ways to deliver it.
4. When ideas stall, attack your hidden assumptions: vary size, material, shape, format, delivery.
5. Expect multiple right answers — then push to find the one that is "way more right."
6. Save convergence (trimming to the final offer) for after you've gone wide.
