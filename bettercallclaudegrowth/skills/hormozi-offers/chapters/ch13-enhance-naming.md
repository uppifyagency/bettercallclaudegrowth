# Chapter 13: Enhancing the Offer: Naming (the MAGIC Formula)

## Core Idea
A Grand Slam Offer earns nothing if no one notices it; the name is the "wrapping paper" that makes prospects feel "this is for ME, it's valuable, and it solves my problem." Use the **MAGIC** formula to name offers (and promotions, and even stack items) so the same underlying offer can pull 2x-10x the response.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **MAGIC Naming Formula**: Build the headline/name from up to five power components:
  - **M — Make a Magnetic "Reason Why"** (Attention/Magnet): the believable reason you're running the promo. Answers "Why this great offer?" or "What's in it for me?" Examples: Free, 88% Off, Giveaway, Spring, Back to School, Grand Opening, New Management, Anniversary, Halloween, New Year.
  - **A — Announce Your Avatar** (Discrimination): call out exactly who it's for (and who it's not). Be as specific as possible, no more. In local markets, go hyper-local — the sub-market, not the city (Towson not Baltimore; Hinsdale not Chicago). Examples: Bee Cave Dentists, Rolling Hills Moms, Salon Owners, Retired Athletes.
  - **G — Give Them a Goal** (Purpose): the prospect's **Dream Outcome** as a word/phrase — event, feeling, experience, or result. More specific and tangible is better. Examples: Pain Free, Celebrity Smile, 1st Place, Double Your Profit, 7 Figure, 100k.
  - **I — Indicate a Time Interval** (Timeline): the duration to expect results. Examples: 4 Hour, 21 Day, 6 Week, 3 Month.
  - **C — Complete With a Container Word** (Method): signals this is a bundled system, not a commodity. Examples: Challenge, Blueprint, Bootcamp, Intensive, Incubator, Masterclass, Accelerator, Sprint, Detox, Reset, Mastermind, Cheatcode.
  - When to use: naming any product, service, or promotion you advertise — and naming each item/bonus in your stack.
  - How: write out names using 3-5 of the components, in any order (not necessarily M-A-G-I-C), pick the punchiest, then **test them** — there's no other way to know what wins.

- **Offer Fatigue Variation Framework** (what to change, in order, lightest to heaviest): 1) the creative (images/video); 2) the body copy; 3) the headline / "wrapper" (rename — e.g. "Free 6 Week Lean Challenge" → "Free 6 Week Tone Challenge"); 4) the seasonality; 5) the duration; 6) the enhancer (your free/discount component); 7) the monetization structure and price points (last resort).
  - When to use: when a working offer's response starts fading.
  - How: exhaust each lighter level before touching the next. Once an offer is monetized, rarely change the machine — rinse and repeat. Only the top few items typically need changing, again and again.

## Key Concepts
- **Wrapping Paper / Wrapper**: the name, copy, and creative exterior of an offer — what you change to refresh it. The actual services, products, pricing, and money model stay the same.
- **The Rename Rule**: when response to a promotion fades, change the name (and creative/copy) and re-launch the same core offer.
- **Container Word**: a word implying a bundled system that can't be commodity-compared (Bootcamp, Blueprint, Intensive).
- **Implicit-Egotism Effect**: people are drawn to things and people that resemble them — why a precisely-named avatar converts.
- **Offer Fatigue**: the decay of response to an offer over years (not months) of repeated exposure — faster in local markets with a small radius.
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)**: for a brick-and-mortar, just its immediate radius; the smaller the radius, the faster offers fatigue.
- **Aces in the Hole**: multiple proven, ready-to-deploy named offers you can rotate when one fades — the ultimate position of marketing power.
- **Control**: the current winning name you test all new name variations against.

## Mental Models
- **Think like a fraternity party planner**: the "reason why" can be literally anything (a guy got his wisdom teeth out) as long as you believe it — so never skip M for lack of a "good" reason.
- **People judge a book by its cover**: lazy naming ruins conversions; the same float-tank session named "Free Six-Week Stress Release Challenge" beats "Float Tank Center Session" every time.
- **Use your entrepreneurial ADD on the wrapper, not the machine**: redirect the itch-to-change toward copy/creative/headlines; changing the underlying business creates operational drag and costs money.
- **Find Time To Rhyme / Use Alliteration**: rhymes and alliteration stick (Six-Pack Fast Track; Debt Detox; Change Your Life Challenge) — a "nice-to-have," never force it.

## Anti-patterns
- **Half-ass / lazy naming**: a generic, commodity-sounding name silently kills conversions even with a great offer.
- **Stuffing all five MAGIC components in**: the name gets too long; balance brevity and specificity (3-5 components).
- **Stating a quantifiable claim with a duration on regulated platforms**: "$10,000 in 10 days" reads as an implied guarantee and gets rejected; reframe to a non-claim ("Make Your First Sale in 10 Days").
- **Declaring fatigue after one exposure**: most people don't notice an offer the first time — refresh creative/hooks/copy long before assuming the offer is dead.
- **Changing the whole machine first**: re-engineering pricing/services to fix flagging leads is the most expensive, last-resort move — do the lighter changes first.
- **Getting emotional about which names win**: some names rocket and others flop for no clear reason; keep testing, don't agonize.

## Key Takeaways
1. Name with **MAGIC** — pick 3-5 components, any order, then write out variants and test; the winner becomes your control.
2. Renaming refreshes a fatigued offer forever — you change only the wrapper, never the underlying offer, services, or pricing.
3. When response fades, change things in order from lightest (creative) to heaviest (monetization); never start at the bottom.
4. Run 2-3 of your best names at once, find the winner, then keep testing new names against it.
5. Apply the MAGIC formula to every item and bonus in your stack to lift perceived value automatically.
6. Go hyper-local with the avatar in local markets, and expect faster fatigue because of the small radius/TAM.
7. Avoid stating a quantifiable outcome + duration where compliance forbids it (it implies a guarantee).
