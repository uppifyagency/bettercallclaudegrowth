# Chapter 7: Create Your Grand Slam Offer I: Problems & Solutions

## Core Idea
Build a Grand Slam Offer by first naming the **Dream Outcome**, then brainstorming EVERY problem/obstacle a prospect believes stands in their way (in insane detail), then reframing each problem into a solution. Resolve all of them and the prospect has no reason left to say no.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 3-Step Offer Build (Part I)**: Dream Outcome → Problems List → Solutions List.
  - When to use: Whenever you're designing an offer from scratch.
  - How:
    1. **Identify Dream Outcome** — sell the destination, not the vehicle ("selling the vacation, not the plane flight"). Make it big with a decreased Time Delay (e.g., "Lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks").
    2. **List Problems** — write down every core thing the customer must do, then every reason they couldn't do it or keep doing it. Think in the sequence the customer experiences each obstacle (before/during/after using your product). The more problems, the better.
    3. **Solutions List** — transform each problem into a solution, then name it (naming covered in Ch13).

- **Problem → Solution Reversal ("How to ___ without ___")**: Reverse each element of an obstacle into solution-oriented language by prefixing "How to" and flipping the negative.
  - When to use: Turning a raw problem list into a deliverables/messaging checklist.
  - How: Ask "What would I need to show someone to solve this problem?" Then rename the obstacle as its removal. Examples from the weight-loss build: "Buying healthy food is expensive" → "How to buy healthy food for less than your current grocery bill"; "Cooking takes too much time" → "How to cook meals in under 5 minutes"; "is unsustainable" → "How to make eating healthy last forever."

- **Four Value-Driver Problem Buckets**: Every obstacle maps to one of the four Value Equation drivers. Use them to prompt your brainstorm:
  - **Dream Outcome** → "This won't be financially worth it."
  - **Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** → "It won't work for me specifically / I won't stick with it / external factors (family, travel) will get in my way." (Most unique and service-specific bucket.)
  - **Effort & Sacrifice** → "This will be too hard, too confusing, I won't like it, I'll suck at it."
  - **Time (Time Delay)** → "This takes too much time / I'm too busy / it'll take too long to work / inconvenient."

## Key Concepts
- **Dream Outcome**: The end state the prospect actually wants (lose weight), not the membership/mechanism they buy to get there.
- **Sell the vacation, not the plane flight**: Position the offer around arriving at the destination and the experience there, never the vehicle.
- **Problem**: Any point of friction or limiting belief a prospect faces immediately before, during, or after using the product — the "next thing they need help with."
- **Insane detail**: The instruction to brainstorm exhaustively — 16 core problems × 2-4 sub-problems = 32-64 total in the example; the more you list, the more value you can build.
- **Solutions List**: The set of "How to ___" reversals — a checklist of exactly what you must solve for the prospect.
- **Value drivers as a guide**: Use the four drivers to spark the problem list, but don't let the buckets constrain you — "just list out everything you can possibly think of."
- **Divergent thinking**: Used here to intuit many solutions from many problems (the open-ended ideation introduced in Ch6).

## Mental Models
- **Think of each problem as permission to buy**: Every solved obstacle gives the prospect one more reason to say yes. If even one need is left unaddressed, it can be the single reason someone doesn't buy.
- **Use the customer's timeline as your brainstorm map**: Walk the sequence — grocery shopping → cooking → eating → exercising — and at each step ask what they need next. New problems manifest the moment one is solved.
- **Reframe "too many problems" as good news**: "The more problems you think of, the more problems you get to solve" — more obstacles = a more valuable, more compelling offer.
- **The four drivers are the only four reasons**: Problems always relate back to Dream Outcome, Likelihood, Effort & Sacrifice, or Time — so repetitive-looking problems are normal and expected.

## Anti-patterns
- **Selling the mechanism instead of the outcome**: Pitching the "$99/mo membership" (the plane flight) fails; people don't want a membership, they want the result.
- **Stopping the problem list too early**: A thin list yields a thin offer. Under-listing leaves unaddressed objections that silently kill the sale.
- **Letting the buckets box you in**: The four value-driver buckets are prompts to get your brain going, not limits — list everything, even off-bucket problems.
- **Getting overwhelmed by the volume**: 32-64 problems is the goal, not a burden; treat the long list as raw material.

## Key Takeaways
1. Start every offer at the Dream Outcome stated as a big result with a short Time Delay (e.g., "20 lbs in 6 weeks").
2. List each core action the customer must take, then every reason (limiting belief or friction) they won't do it or won't sustain it — go for sheer quantity.
3. Use the four Value-Equation drivers (Dream Outcome, Likelihood, Effort & Sacrifice, Time) as prompts to surface problems across before/during/after the purchase.
4. Convert each problem into a solution with the "How to [achieve X] without [obstacle]" reversal — this becomes your deliverables checklist.
5. Commit to solving EVERY problem; a single unresolved obstacle can be the reason a prospect walks.
6. Don't be fancy — "just get all the problems down then turn them into solutions."
