# Chapter 4: Charge What It's Worth (Premium Pricing)

## Core Idea
Charge a premium, never compete on price: raising price (after raising value) widens the Price-to-Value Discrepancy in the customer's favor while triggering the Virtuous Cycle of Price — higher prices increase client commitment, which produces better results, which justifies even higher prices. The objective of a business is not to get the most customers; it's to make the most money while delivering the most value.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Virtuous Cycle of Price**: raising your price sets off a self-reinforcing loop — Increase clients' emotional investment → Increase perceived value → Increase clients' results (they're invested, so they follow through) → Attract the best clients (easiest to satisfy, cheaper to fulfill) → Multiply your margin (money to invest in systems, talent, experience, scale) → which funds even better fulfillment → which justifies charging more again.
  - When to use: anytime you doubt a price, or feel market pressure to discount. Hormozi reinforces it constantly because "the forces of the marketplace will grate on your belief system."
  - How: raise price ONLY after you have sufficiently increased value, so the client still gets a deal (e.g., $100,000 of value for $10,000 = "money at a discount").

- **Vicious Cycle (low price)**: the exact inverse and the road to destruction — lowering price decreases emotional investment → decreases perceived value ("can't be good if it's cheap") → decreases results → attracts the worst clients (never satisfied until it's free) → destroys margin (no money to provide an exceptional experience, hire well, or scale). Result: "your world sucks" and your service probably sucks because you're squeezing blood from a stone.
  - When to use: as a warning model. Discounting is a one-way road — you can only go down to $0, but price can go infinitely high upward.

## Key Concepts

- **Price-to-Value Discrepancy**: the gap between PRICE (what they give) and VALUE (what they get); people buy to get a deal, and you want this gap as large as possible in the buyer's favor.
- **"Money at a discount"**: framing a Grand Slam Offer as selling, e.g., $100,000 of value for $10,000 so the buyer feels they're buying money cheaply.
- **Premium pricing**: deliberately pricing far above the market — not slightly above, but so high the buyer thinks "there must be something entirely different going on here."
- **Category of one / monopoly**: when your price is so much higher you escape comparison; in this new perceived marketplace you are a monopoly and can make monopoly profits.
- **Higher Price = Higher Value (literally)**: raising price increases the value the consumer perceives without changing the product at all (the blind wine taste-test: same wine, rated by price).
- **Conviction**: the unshakable belief — earned through repeated delivery and experience — that lets you charge "someone's entire year's salary"; your conviction must be stronger than their skepticism.
- **"Those who pay the most, pay the most attention"**: a price that stings forces focus and follow-through, which produces better client results.
- **Market efficiency / race to the bottom**: pricing where the market is means competitors keep offering "a little more for a little less" until everyone barely survives.

## Mental Models

- **Use the "would YOU buy it?" test when a price feels too high.** Hormozi's dad accepted $42K the instant he saw the math: $239K return, 15 hrs/week, 11 months, $0 upfront. If the value, time, effort, and risk math makes a skeptic say yes, the price is right — discrepancy, not the absolute number, is what sells.
- **Think of price as a value-creation lever, not just a number.** The wine experiment proves perceived value rises with price alone — so charging more literally makes your product "better" to the buyer.
- **Think of a high price as a tool for the CLIENT'S benefit (the moral case).** If results require the client to do the work, more investment = more follow-through = better outcomes. So if you care about clients, make it sting a little. Premium pricing is therefore a moral choice, not just a smart one.
- **Use "make the most money," not "get the most customers," as your scoreboard.** Getting people to buy is not the objective of a business; making money is.

## Anti-patterns

- **Pricing off the market**: look at competitors → take the average → go slightly below to stay "competitive" → offer "more for less." Fails because the competitors you're copying are dead broke, and "more for less" just feeds the race to the bottom toward perfect efficiency where everyone barely survives.
- **Discounting to win the sale**: the simplest way to widen the gap, and usually the wrong one — it ignites the Vicious Cycle and there's "no strategic benefit to being the second cheapest" (Dan Kennedy).
- **Charging premium without delivery**: trying to shortcut the real work. Big-ticket prices demand a product that delivers and conviction earned from doing it many times; bluffing fails.

## Reference Tables

| Lower your price (Vicious Cycle) | Raise your price (Virtuous Cycle) |
|---|---|
| Decrease clients' emotional investment | Increase clients' emotional investment |
| Decrease perceived value | Increase perceived value |
| Decrease clients' results | Increase clients' results |
| Attract the worst clients (never satisfied) | Attract the best clients (easiest to satisfy, cost less to fulfill) |
| Destroy margin (no money to invest) | Multiply margin (invest in systems, talent, scale) |

Buying logic (the deal): **Buy when VALUE > PRICE.** The moment VALUE dips below PRICE, buying stops. Goal: maximize the VALUE − PRICE gap, then raise price into that gap.

## Key Takeaways
1. Charge a premium — the premium is what funds the ability to do things no competitor can, which is what actually makes clients successful.
2. Raise price only AFTER raising value, so the client still gets a massive deal; that preserved gap keeps the Virtuous Cycle spinning.
3. Don't compete on price unless you can cut costs to ~1/10th of rivals — price can go infinitely up but only down to zero.
4. Price so high you create a category of one ("there must be something different here"), not slightly above market.
5. Make it sting: those who pay the most pay the most attention, follow through, and get the best results — so a high price is the more caring choice.
6. 99% of businesses need to raise prices to grow, not lower them — "Profit is oxygen. It fuels the fire of growth."
7. To hold a premium price, build conviction by actually delivering results repeatedly; experience is what lets you ask for big-ticket prices without flinching.
