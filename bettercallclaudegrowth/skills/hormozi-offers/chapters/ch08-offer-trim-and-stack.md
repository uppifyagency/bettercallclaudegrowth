# Chapter 8: Create Your Grand Slam Offer II: Trim & Stack

## Core Idea
Turn your raw list of solution delivery vehicles into a sellable, high-margin bundle by trimming the costly/low-value pieces and stacking the high-value ones — so the offer is easy to sell AND cheap to fulfill, becoming uncomparable to anything else in the market.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Sales to Fulfillment Continuum**: Every business sits on a continuum between ease of fulfillment and ease of sales. Do more for the client → easier to SELL but harder/costlier to FULFILL. Do less → easier to fulfill but harder to sell. The goal is the sweet spot: sells very well AND is easy to fulfill.
  - When to use: Whenever deciding how much to do for a client; especially when launching a first **Grand Slam Offer** vs. scaling later.
  - How: Start by over-delivering like crazy to win cash flow ("Create flow. Monetize flow. Then add friction."). Generate demand → get yeses → only THEN add friction or offer less for the same price. Use early cash to build systems that deliver the same value at lower cost.

- **Step #4 — Solution Delivery Vehicles ("The How") / "The Cube"**: For each problem, divergently brainstorm every possible way you could deliver the solution. Group by how many you deliver to at once: one-on-one, small group, one-to-many.
  - When to use: After listing problems→solutions (Ch7), before trimming.
  - How (the Product Delivery Cheat Codes — vary one axis at a time): (1) Level of personal attention — 1-on-1 / small group / one-to-many; (2) Level of effort expected from them — DIY (do it themselves), DWY (do it with them), DFY (done for them); (3) If live, the medium — in-person / phone / email / text / Zoom / chat; (4) If recorded, the format — audio / video / written; (5) Speed of reply — days, hours, 24/7, within 5 min, etc.; (6) The 10x-to-1/10th test (see Mental Models).

- **Step #5 — Trim & Stack**: Reduce the giant list to only the highest-value, lowest-cost items, then bundle them into one irresistible deliverable.
  - When to use: After the Step #4 brainstorm produces a "monster list."
  - How: See Reference Tables.

## Key Concepts
- **Trim**: Removing offer items, done in strict order — first cut HIGH-cost/LOW-value, then cut LOW-cost/LOW-value.
- **Stack**: Bundling the surviving high-value pieces into a single high-value deliverable so total perceived value dwarfs the price.
- **One-to-many solutions**: Delivery vehicles with the biggest discrepancy between cost and value (high one-time creation cost, near-zero marginal cost). The reason software is so valuable.
- **DIY / DWY / DFY**: The effort spectrum — Do-It-Yourself, Done-With-You, Done-For-You. DFY sells easier but costs most to fulfill.
- **Solve every perceived problem**: A single unsolved "one thing" can be the reason someone doesn't buy; resolve every obstacle a buyer believes they'll hit.
- **High-value asset**: A created-once deliverable (templates, calculators, meal plans, even a book) that produces high-margin profit in perpetuity — the bedrock of a scalable model.
- **Price-to-Value Discrepancy**: The aim — "I get all this, for only that?" High price, but a steal for the value.

## Mental Models
- **Use "Create flow. Monetize flow. Then add friction." when** building or launching: prove demand and get cash first, optimize fulfillment second — you can't know if your offer is good with zero flow coming in.
- **Use the 10x-to-1/10th test when** stuck for ideas: ask "If they paid me 10x (or $100,000), what would I provide?" and "If they paid 1/10th and I had to make it MORE valuable, how?" — stretching both directions yields wildly different solutions.
- **Think of high-cost (1-on-1, small group) delivery as a scarce resource**: reserve it only for big value-adds. If a lower-cost alternative creates the same value, use that instead.
- **Don't get romantic about HOW you solve a problem**: take "one step back at a time" from the ideal-but-unscalable version until you reach a cost/time commitment you can live with — or massively raise the price so it's worth it.

## Anti-patterns
- **Optimizing fulfillment before you have cash flow**: kills your ability to learn what customers actually need and starves the business.
- **Refusing to solve a prospect's "one thing"**: being a stickler for your preferred method ("do it my way or not at all") loses sales you could have closed with a simple template or guide.
- **Keeping high-cost/low-value items in the bundle**: bloats your fulfillment cost and destroys margin without making the offer more sellable.
- **Presenting the bundle the same way regardless of channel**: how you present stacked items should differ for one-on-one vs. one-to-many sales.

## Reference Tables

**Trim & Stack — the value/cost grid (what to cut, what to keep)**

| | Low Value | High Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Low Cost** | Cut SECOND | KEEP (ideal) |
| **High Cost** | Cut FIRST | KEEP (reserve for big value-adds) |

To judge "high value," run each item through the **Value Equation** — does it: (1) get them financially valued benefit (**Dream Outcome**), (2) make them believe they'll succeed (**Perceived Likelihood of Achievement**), (3) feel achievable with much less **Effort and Sacrifice**, (4) deliver the result with far less **Time Delay**.

**The full 5-step Grand Slam Offer build (Ch7 → Ch8)**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Identify the prospect's **Dream Outcome** |
| 2 | List every obstacle/problem they'll hit (= value opportunities) |
| 3 | Restate each obstacle as a solution |
| 4 | Brainstorm all delivery vehicles for each solution (divergent) |
| 5a | Trim to only highest-value, lowest-cost items |
| 5b | Stack the survivors into one ultimate high-value deliverable |

## Key Takeaways
1. Do Step #4 once: list every conceivable way to deliver each solution, grouped by 1-on-1 / small group / one-to-many — this is high-leverage thinking you reuse for years.
2. Trim in order: kill high-cost/low-value first, then low-cost/low-value; keep the two "high-value" quadrants.
3. Favor high-value one-to-many vehicles — biggest cost-to-value gap; pay the one-time creation cost to earn high-margin profit "in perpetuity."
4. Solve EVERY perceived problem; one unaddressed objection is enough to lose a sale.
5. A stacked bundle does three things: solves all perceived problems, gives you conviction it's one-of-a-kind, and makes you impossible to compare to the competitor down the street.
6. When you stack enough value (e.g., $4,351 total value sold for $599), the buyer makes a value-based — not price-based — decision, escaping commoditization.
